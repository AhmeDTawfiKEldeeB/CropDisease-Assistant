# Late Blight - Tomato

**Pathogen:** Phytophthora infestans  
**Type:** Oomycete  
**Host:** Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Late Blight is a disease caused by the oomycete pathogen Phytophthora infestans that attacks tomato and potato plants in cool, moist conditions. It is arguably the most historically significant plant disease in the world, being responsible for the Irish Potato Famine of the 1840s. On tomato, it is among the most destructive diseases possible, capable of destroying an entire field within days under ideal conditions. The disease affects all above-ground parts of the plant, causing large, rapidly expanding lesions on leaves, stems, and fruit that have a characteristic water-soaked, greasy appearance and, under humid conditions, a white downy sporulation on the underside of affected tissue.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Late Blight is rated as the highest danger tomato disease. Under cool, moist conditions, it can destroy a tomato field completely within one to two weeks of first appearance. The speed of destruction is unmatched by any other common tomato pathogen. Yield losses of one hundred percent have been documented in unmanaged fields during epidemic years. The disease can spread from a single infected plant to an entire field within days during favorable weather, and it can spread between fields over distances of several kilometers via airborne sporangia.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
The incubation period is three to seven days under optimal cool, moist conditions. Under highly favorable conditions of temperatures around fifteen to twenty degrees Celsius with prolonged leaf wetness, the disease can advance from first visible symptoms to complete field destruction within seven to fourteen days. The speed of spread is the defining characteristic of Late Blight epidemics and makes rapid response absolutely critical when the disease is first detected.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
Phytophthora infestans spreads primarily through airborne sporangia that are produced in massive quantities from infected tissue under cool, humid conditions. Sporangia can travel tens of kilometers on wind currents, making the disease capable of appearing in fields far from obvious inoculum sources. In the United States, the primary source of inoculum is infected transplants produced in southern growing regions and shipped north. The pathogen also survives in infected potato tubers left in soil from the previous season, which serve as a primary inoculum source in potato-growing regions. It requires free water on tissue surfaces for spore germination and infection.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Phytophthora infestans is an oomycete, not a true fungus, though it resembles fungi in many ways. It is a diploid organism that produces both asexual sporangia and, when two compatible mating types are present, sexual oospores. The asexual sporangia can function as direct infection propagules or can release biflagellate zoospores under cool temperatures, each capable of independent infection. The pathogen is highly variable, with new genotypes arising through sexual recombination and selection. Fungicide resistance has developed in many populations to key fungicide classes.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On leaves, Late Blight causes large, irregular, water-soaked lesions that appear greasy or dark green and expand rapidly. Under humid conditions, a white to grayish downy growth of sporangiophores and sporangia is visible on the underside of lesions. Lesions turn brown and papery as tissue dies. On stems, dark brown to black lesions cause rapid collapse and death of the stem above the infected area. Stem infections can kill the entire shoot within days. On fruit, firm, dark brown to grayish-green lesions develop that have a slightly greasy or water-soaked appearance. White sporulation may appear on fruit surfaces under humid conditions. Infected fruit collapse rapidly and become unmarketable.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Late Blight thrives in cool, moist conditions with temperatures between ten and twenty-four degrees Celsius, with optimum infection around fifteen to twenty degrees. The pathogen requires prolonged periods of leaf wetness for spore germination and infection. Foggy, cloudy weather with temperatures in this range is considered high risk. Hot, dry weather is inhibitory and can slow or stop epidemic development. The disease is less of a concern in hot, dry summer climates but can develop rapidly when cool periods with rain or heavy dews occur.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
The cycle begins with inoculum from infected transplants, potato tubers, or airborne sporangia from distant sources. Under cool, moist conditions, infected tissue rapidly produces sporangia that are dispersed by wind. New infections develop within three to seven days and produce the next generation of sporangia. This cycle repeats continuously under favorable conditions, allowing exponential increase in disease severity. Hot, dry weather interrupts the cycle. Sexual oospores, when present, allow long-term survival in soil.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
Prevention is the most important aspect of management, as the speed of Late Blight development makes reactive management very difficult. Using certified disease-free transplants from reputable sources is critical. Avoiding overhead irrigation and reducing leaf wetness periods limits infection events. Removing and destroying infected tissue immediately upon detection can slow spread within a field. No commercial tomato variety is fully resistant to all strains of Phytophthora infestans, but varieties with partial resistance or specific resistance genes such as Ph-3 provide meaningful reduction in disease severity and slow epidemic development significantly.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Fungicide management must be preventive, as curative activity of even the best fungicides is very limited once Late Blight is established. Applications should begin when weather conditions become favorable or when Late Blight is detected in the region, whichever comes first, and not wait for disease to appear in the field. Fungicide classes with activity against oomycetes include mandipropamid, dimethomorph, fluopicolide, propamocarb, mancozeb, and copper compounds. Strobilurins do not have reliable activity against Phytophthora infestans. Applications should be made on five to seven day intervals during cool, wet weather. Resistance management is critical, as resistance to several key fungicide classes is widespread in many Phytophthora infestans populations.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
When Late Blight is confirmed anywhere in the region, apply a preventive fungicide to all tomato fields immediately regardless of whether disease is visible. When first lesions appear in a field, immediately remove and destroy all infected tissue and apply a systemic oomycide within twenty-four hours. During cool, foggy weather, apply fungicides on five-day intervals. If disease cannot be controlled and is spreading rapidly, consider early harvest of marketable fruit before complete field destruction occurs.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Scout fields daily during cool, moist weather and during regional Late Blight alerts. Examine all parts of the plant including lower leaves, stems, and fruit. During foggy or rainy cool periods, inspect with particular intensity as conditions can shift from low to high risk rapidly. Use regional Late Blight forecasting systems and alerts from extension services to anticipate high-risk periods. Confirm suspicious samples by laboratory diagnosis, as several other diseases can resemble Late Blight.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Late Blight of tomato is most severe in cool, moist tomato-growing regions including the northeastern United States, northern Europe, the Andean regions of South America, and highland areas in tropical countries where cool nights and wet conditions favor the pathogen. It is a sporadic but catastrophic threat in all tomato-growing regions where cool, wet conditions can occur during the growing season.
