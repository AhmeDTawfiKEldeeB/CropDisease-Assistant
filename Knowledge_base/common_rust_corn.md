# Common Rust - Corn
Pathogen: Puccinia sorghi | Type: Fungal (Rust) | Host: Corn / Maize (Zea mays)


## Definition

Common Rust is a fungal disease of corn caused by Puccinia sorghi, a rust fungus that produces distinctive brick-red powdery pustules scattered across both surfaces of leaves. It is one of the most widespread corn diseases globally, present wherever corn is grown. While it appears alarming due to the vivid coloration of the pustules, it is rarely devastating on field corn when resistant hybrids are used. On sweet corn and highly susceptible hybrids, however, it can cause significant defoliation and yield loss. The pathogen is an obligate parasite that requires living host tissue to survive and cannot persist in soil or dead debris.


## Severity and Danger Level

The danger level of Common Rust is moderate on field corn and rises to high on sweet corn and susceptible hybrids. The disease rarely kills plants directly but reduces photosynthesis and impairs grain fill. Yield losses on susceptible field corn hybrids typically range from five to twenty percent, while losses on sweet corn can reach forty percent. Resistant field corn varieties experience minimal loss. The economic impact is moderate on field corn and high on sweet corn and popcorn. Once infected, tissue cannot be cured and retains pustules for the rest of the season.


## Time to Spread

Under optimal cool and humid conditions, Common Rust takes seven to fourteen days from infection to visible symptoms. The fungus produces new spore generations every seven to ten days, allowing the disease to cycle rapidly through a field. From the first pustules to severe infection takes two to four weeks in cool, humid weather. Spread stops quickly when temperatures become hot and dry, which is an important characteristic that distinguishes it from more aggressive diseases.


## How It Spreads

Common Rust spreads almost exclusively through wind-dispersed urediospores that travel from infected fields, often originating hundreds of kilometers away in tropical or subtropical regions where the fungus survives year-round. In temperate climates, the fungus cannot overwinter on corn residue and must be reintroduced each season from warmer areas. Once spores land on corn leaves, they germinate in the presence of moisture, penetrate through stomata without requiring any wound, and establish infection. Within a field, each pustule produces millions of new spores that spread to neighboring plants through wind. Rotation and tillage do not reduce risk because inoculum arrives from distant sources, not from local residue.


## Pathogen Biology

Puccinia sorghi is a basidiomycete rust fungus with a complex life cycle involving two hosts. On corn, it produces urediospores that drive epidemic spread and teliospores that form late in the season. Teliospores infect the alternate host, Oxalis species or wood sorrel, on which sexual recombination produces aeciospores that can re-infect corn. In tropical and subtropical regions this full cycle maintains year-round populations. In temperate regions the alternate host cycle is less epidemiologically important. The fungus is an obligate biotroph, meaning it grows only in living tissue and cannot be cultured on artificial media. This makes it difficult to study in the laboratory but also means it dies with its host in cold climates.


## Symptoms

On leaves, Common Rust produces small, circular to elongate pustules that are brick-red to cinnamon-brown in color and appear on both the upper and lower leaf surfaces. This bilateral distribution is an important diagnostic feature that distinguishes Common Rust from Southern Rust, which produces pustules mainly on the upper surface. The pustules rupture the leaf epidermis and release a powdery mass of brick-red urediospores. Later in the season, pustules darken to brown or black as teliospores replace urediospores. In severe infections, leaves turn yellow and then brown and die prematurely. Sheaths and husks may also be infected in very susceptible hybrids under heavy pressure.


## Favorable Conditions

Common Rust thrives in cool temperatures between sixteen and twenty-three degrees Celsius, with the optimum around twenty degrees. Dew, fog, or rain on leaf surfaces is required for spore germination. The disease is most severe from the V10 growth stage through silking, and late-planted corn is at highest risk because it passes through the susceptible stage when inoculum levels are at their peak. High-elevation tropical corn production is particularly vulnerable. Cool nights with heavy dew are a strong predictor of active infection.


## Disease Cycle

Each season in temperate regions, urediospores arrive via wind from southern or tropical areas. They land on corn leaves, germinate in moisture, and penetrate through stomata. Pustules develop within seven to fourteen days and release new urediospores. Multiple infection cycles repeat throughout the season, each cycle seven to ten days long. Late in the season, dark teliospores form within pustules. In regions where both A1 and A2 mating types coexist, sexual recombination on the Oxalis alternate host generates new races capable of overcoming host resistance genes.


## Treatment and Management

The most effective management strategy is planting hybrids with strong rust resistance ratings. Hybrids rated six or above on a nine-point scale tolerate substantial rust pressure without significant yield loss. Quantitative or partial resistance, which is controlled by multiple genes, is more durable than single-gene resistance because it is harder for new fungal races to overcome completely.

Cultural practices have limited value against Common Rust because inoculum arrives from wind rather than from local fields. Planting early allows the crop to pass through the most susceptible vegetative stages before peak inoculum arrives. Removing volunteer corn plants reduces early-season infection sources. Balanced nutrition, particularly adequate phosphorus and potassium, supports plant tolerance.

Fungicide applications are economically justified on susceptible hybrids and sweet corn when disease reaches the ear leaf or above at or before tasseling. Effective fungicide classes include triazoles such as propiconazole and tebuconazole, strobilurins such as azoxystrobin and pyraclostrobin, and premix products combining SDHI and triazole chemistries. The optimal timing is between the VT and R1 growth stages. A single well-timed application is usually sufficient. On sweet corn the threshold is much lower and fungicide should be applied as soon as rust appears before or at tasseling. Applications after the R3 milk stage have very limited economic return on field corn. Modes of action should be alternated to reduce resistance risk.

Biological control options currently have minimal practical value against corn rust. Bacillus-based biofungicides show little activity, and no commercially effective biological product exists for this disease.


## Quick Actions

Before planting, choose a hybrid with a rust resistance rating of six or higher out of nine. During early vegetative stages when pustules are present, monitor closely but do not spray because plants can tolerate early rust without economic loss. When approaching tasseling with rust visible on the ear leaf, apply a triazole or premix fungicide immediately. On sweet corn at any stage with rust visible, apply fungicide promptly because sweet corn tolerates much less pressure. After tasseling in a severely infected field, apply fungicide only if the crop is before the R3 dough stage.


## Scouting and Monitoring

Begin scouting at the V8 growth stage and intensify from V10 through R2. Sample at least twenty plants per field at multiple locations throughout the field. Record the percentage of plants showing rust and note the highest leaf position where pustules are present. The key threshold for fungicide application is disease on the ear leaf or above at tasseling. Monitor weather for cool nights below twenty degrees Celsius combined with dew, which signals high infection risk. Regional rust forecasting maps are available in some areas and can guide scouting priorities.


## Geographic Distribution

Common Rust is present globally wherever corn is grown. It is most severe in tropical highlands such as East Africa, Central America, and the Andean region of South America. In temperate regions of North America and Europe, severity varies year to year depending on how early inoculum arrives from southern source regions and whether cool, humid weather follows. Late-planted fields and high-elevation areas within any region are at the greatest risk.


## Frequently Asked Questions

Is Common Rust the same as Southern Rust? No. Southern Rust is caused by Puccinia polysora and is more aggressive. Common Rust pustules appear on both leaf surfaces while Southern Rust pustules are mostly on the upper surface. Southern Rust pustules are also more orange in color and smaller in size.

The rust appeared suddenly overnight. Is that normal? Yes. Wind-carried spores can arrive in large numbers overnight, and cool, dewy nights cause rapid infection. What looks like a sudden outbreak is often the visible result of infections that occurred seven to fourteen days earlier.

My field was rotated away from corn. Why do I still have rust? Because Common Rust inoculum comes from wind, not from local soil or residue. Rotation does not protect against this disease.

Can Common Rust cause stalk rot? Indirectly. Severe rust reduces photosynthesis and forces the plant to remobilize stalk carbohydrates to fill grain, weakening stalks and increasing susceptibility to Gibberella and Fusarium stalk rots and subsequent lodging.

Does rust affect grain quality or safety? Rust does not produce mycotoxins. It reduces yield and test weight but does not make grain unsafe for food or feed.
