# Leaf Scorch - Strawberry

**Pathogen:** Diplocarpon earlianum  
**Type:** Fungal  
**Host:** Strawberry (Fragaria × ananassa)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Leaf Scorch is a fungal disease caused by Diplocarpon earlianum that attacks strawberry plants in cool, wet conditions. It is one of the most common foliar diseases of strawberry worldwide, causing small, irregular purple to reddish-purple spots on leaves that coalesce and give the leaf a scorched appearance. In severe cases, entire leaves are killed and plants are significantly weakened, reducing fruit yield and plant vigor. The disease is particularly problematic in matted row systems where plant density is high and canopy airflow is reduced, creating ideal conditions for fungal spread and infection.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Leaf Scorch is rated as a moderate danger disease. It rarely causes complete crop failure in a single season but can cause significant reduction in plant vigor and productivity when infections are severe and repeated over multiple seasons. Perennial plantings that are not renewed frequently suffer cumulative damage that reduces yield over time. The disease is most economically significant in regions with cool, wet springs and summers that favor repeated infection cycles throughout the growing season.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
The incubation period ranges from five to twelve days depending on temperature and humidity. The disease can produce multiple infection cycles throughout the growing season under favorable conditions. The most rapid spread occurs during cool, wet spring weather when young leaves are emerging and the pathogen has overwintered inoculum available. Summer infections continue at a slower rate but accumulate through the season.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
Diplocarpon earlianum overwinters on infected leaves and plant debris in the strawberry planting. In spring, rainfall triggers the release of conidia from overwintered lesions on old leaves. These spores are spread by rain splash to new leaves. Once established, new lesions produce conidia throughout the growing season that continue to spread through the planting during wet periods. Dense canopy plantings with poor airflow accumulate moisture and facilitate rapid secondary spread. Old infected leaves left in the planting are the primary source of inoculum season to season.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Diplocarpon earlianum is an ascomycete fungus that produces both asexual conidia and sexual ascospores. The asexual stage is the primary means of spread during the growing season. The fungus sporulates within acervuli that form inside leaf lesions and release conidia during wet weather. The sexual stage provides overwintering persistence and genetic recombination. The fungus does not persist in soil but survives in infected plant tissue.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On leaves, Leaf Scorch causes small, irregular spots that begin as reddish-purple flecks and enlarge to form spots with purplish-red margins and tan to gray centers. Individual spots range from two to five millimeters. As multiple spots merge, the leaf develops a scorched or blighted appearance with large areas of dead tissue surrounded by purple-red borders. Severely infected leaves dry out and die but often remain attached to the plant. On petioles and runners, small elongated lesions may develop that are less common but contribute to overall plant stress when severe. On fruit caps, reddish lesions may occasionally develop, though fruit infection is not the primary economic concern of this disease.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Leaf Scorch thrives in cool, wet conditions with temperatures between fifteen and twenty degrees Celsius for optimal infection. The fungus requires free water on leaf surfaces for spore germination and infection. Prolonged periods of leaf wetness from rain, dew, or overhead irrigation promote rapid disease development. Dense plantings that maintain high humidity within the canopy are particularly favorable. The disease is most severe in spring and early summer when conditions are cool and wet.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
The cycle begins with inoculum overwintering on infected leaves in the planting. Spring rains activate overwintered conidia that infect new leaves. Infected leaves develop lesions that produce new conidia within one to two weeks. Multiple cycles of infection and sporulation occur throughout the season during wet periods. At the end of the season, infected leaf tissue remains in the planting and serves as inoculum for the following spring.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
Cultural management is critical and begins with renovating matted row plantings after each harvest season. Renovation involving mowing, thinning, and narrowing of rows reduces canopy density, improves airflow, and removes heavily infected tissue. Removing old leaves and plant debris reduces overwintering inoculum. Avoiding overhead irrigation in favor of drip irrigation eliminates leaf wetness associated with irrigation. Proper plant spacing at establishment reduces canopy density in annual systems. Variety selection plays an important role. Some strawberry varieties such as Chandler and Jewel show moderate resistance, while others show high susceptibility. Selecting varieties with better resistance ratings reduces disease pressure in regions where Leaf Scorch is consistently problematic.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Chemical control is justified in plantings with a history of the disease and during cool, wet seasons. Fungicide classes used include multi-site protectants such as captan and thiram, and single-site fungicides including triazoles such as myclobutanil and SDHI products. Applications should begin in early spring at leaf emergence and continue on ten to fourteen day intervals during favorable weather. Fungicide programs should be integrated with renovation practices for maximum effectiveness. Resistance management through mode of action rotation is important given the multiple generations of the pathogen produced each season.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
After harvest, mow the planting, narrow the rows, and remove all old leaf tissue from the planting area. When first spots are detected in spring, apply a protective fungicide and increase monitoring frequency. During cool, wet spring weather, maintain ten-day spray intervals without gaps. When severe defoliation is observed in older plantings, assess whether renovation or replanting is more economical than continued management. Before planting new beds, select resistant varieties and certified disease-free transplants.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Scout plantings weekly from early spring leaf emergence through the end of the growing season. Examine the older leaves in the lower canopy first, as disease typically appears there earliest. Assess the percentage of leaves with lesions and the severity of individual lesion development. Older matted row plantings should be scouted more intensively than new annual plantings. Monitor weather conditions for cool, wet periods that favor rapid disease increase.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Leaf Scorch of strawberry is present in all major strawberry-producing regions worldwide. It is most severe in cool, humid regions with wet spring weather including the northeastern United States, northern Europe, and New Zealand. It is less damaging in warm, dry climates such as California where conditions do not favor prolonged leaf wetness.
