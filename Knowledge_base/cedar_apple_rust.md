# Cedar Apple Rust - Apple

**Pathogen:** Gymnosporangium juniperi-virginianae  
**Type:** Fungal  
**Host:** Apple (Malus domestica)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Cedar Apple Rust is a fungal disease caused by Gymnosporangium juniperi-virginianae that requires two different host plants to complete its life cycle: eastern red cedar or other juniper species and apple or crabapple. It is a significant disease in apple-producing regions where juniper hosts are present, causing bright orange lesions on leaves and fruit that reduce photosynthesis, cause premature defoliation, and render fruit unmarketable. The disease is unique among apple diseases in requiring an alternate host to complete its biological cycle, making proximity to juniper trees a key risk factor.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Cedar Apple Rust is rated as a moderate to high danger disease. It rarely destroys an entire crop but can cause significant defoliation in susceptible varieties, weakening trees and reducing productivity. Heavy fruit infection renders apples unmarketable due to distortion and surface lesions. The severity of the disease in any given year depends heavily on the proximity of juniper hosts, spring weather conditions, and variety susceptibility. Repeated annual defoliation of susceptible trees can reduce long-term orchard productivity.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
Infection of apple tissue occurs exclusively during a narrow window in spring corresponding to the release of basidiospores from orange gelatinous telial horns on infected cedars. This window typically coincides with apple bloom and early leaf development. The incubation period on apple is eight to fourteen days. Secondary spread from apple to apple does not occur; the fungus must return to a juniper host to complete its cycle. The entire apple infection season is typically compressed into a two to four week period in spring.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
The fungus overwinters as galls on eastern red cedar and other junipers. In spring, during wet weather, the galls produce bright orange gelatinous telial horns that release basidiospores. These spores are windborne and can travel significant distances, up to several kilometers, to infect apple leaves and fruit. Infected apple tissue later produces spores that infect junipers in late summer, completing the cycle. Apple tissue cannot infect other apple trees, and juniper tissue cannot infect other junipers. The alternating host requirement is an absolute biological necessity for the pathogen.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Gymnosporangium juniperi-virginianae is a basidiomycete rust fungus with an obligate two-host life cycle. On juniper, it forms brown woody galls that produce bright orange telial horns during spring rains. Basidiospores released from these horns infect apple tissue. On apple, the fungus produces pycnia and aecia on leaves and fruit. Aeciospores produced on apple infect juniper tissue in late summer, where new galls develop over one to two years before the cycle repeats. The fungus cannot complete its life cycle on a single host.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On apple leaves, Cedar Apple Rust causes bright yellow-orange spots on the upper surface that are highly distinctive and visible from a distance. As lesions age, small orange tube-like structures called aecia develop on the lower leaf surface directly beneath the upper lesion. Severely infected leaves may drop prematurely. On apple fruit, bright orange to yellow lesions develop on the surface, often near the calyx end. Infected fruit become distorted and are unmarketable. Aecia may also develop on fruit surfaces. On junipers, round brown galls form on branches that can range from pea-sized to golf-ball-sized. In spring, the galls produce bright orange gelatinous telial horns that are unmistakable and highly visible during wet weather.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Cedar Apple Rust requires wet, cool to mild spring weather coinciding with the period when telial horns are active on cedar galls. Infection of apple occurs most readily at temperatures between ten and twenty-one degrees Celsius with leaf wetness periods of at least four hours. Orchards situated near or downwind of large stands of eastern red cedar or ornamental junipers face the highest risk. The disease is less severe in years with dry spring weather during the bloom period.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
The cycle begins with mature galls on junipers that have developed over one to two seasons. During spring rains, telial horns emerge and release basidiospores that infect young apple leaves and fruit. Lesions develop on apple tissue over two to four weeks. In midsummer, aeciospores produced on the underside of apple leaves are released and carried by wind to infect juniper tissue, where new galls begin forming. These galls mature over the following one to two years before the cycle repeats.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
Removing juniper hosts from the vicinity of the orchard is the most effective long-term cultural control measure but is often impractical given the wide dispersal of basidiospores. Selecting rust-resistant apple varieties is the most sustainable management strategy where variety flexibility exists. Variety selection is critical. Resistant varieties such as Liberty, Redfree, and Enterprise have excellent resistance and require little or no fungicide for rust control. Highly susceptible varieties include Golden Delicious, Jonathan, and Rome.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Chemical control focuses on protecting apple tissue during the primary infection window from pink bud through first cover. Fungicide applications must be timed to cover the period of basidiospore release from cedar galls. Effective fungicide classes include triazoles such as myclobutanil and fenbuconazole, and strobilurins such as trifloxystrobin. Applications should begin at pink bud and continue through petal fall on a seven to ten day schedule during wet weather. Protectant fungicides alone are less effective given the narrow and sometimes unpredictable infection window; systemic fungicides with kickback activity are preferred.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
In late winter and early spring, inspect nearby juniper trees for brown galls to assess inoculum pressure. When telial horns become visible on cedars during spring rains, begin fungicide applications on apple immediately. At pink bud and bloom, maintain fungicide coverage without gaps during wet weather. When first orange spots appear on leaves, apply a systemic fungicide within forty-eight hours. After harvest, assess defoliation severity and note juniper gall density for planning the following season.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Monitor nearby juniper trees from late winter for gall development. Once galls are identified, track weather conditions in spring for the onset of telial horn production. Scout apple leaves and fruit weekly from pink bud through petal fall. The window of active spore release from cedars can be tracked using online rust prediction tools available in several eastern states.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Cedar Apple Rust is most severe in the eastern United States where eastern red cedar is abundant. It is present wherever susceptible apple varieties and juniper hosts occur in proximity, including parts of Canada and isolated regions where ornamental junipers are planted near orchards. The disease does not occur in regions where juniper alternate hosts are absent.
