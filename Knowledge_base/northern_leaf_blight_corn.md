# Northern Leaf Blight - Corn

**Pathogen:** Exserohilum turcicum  
**Type:** Fungal  
**Host:** Corn (Zea mays)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Northern Leaf Blight is a fungal disease caused by Exserohilum turcicum that attacks corn in cool, humid growing conditions. It is one of the most widespread and economically important foliar diseases of corn worldwide, capable of causing significant yield losses in susceptible hybrids under favorable weather conditions. The disease is characterized by large, cigar-shaped gray-green to tan lesions on the leaves that reduce photosynthetic capacity and, in severe cases, cause premature death of the upper canopy. It is most damaging when infection occurs before or during silking, as this is the period of maximum leaf area and grain fill demand.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Northern Leaf Blight is rated as a moderate to high danger disease. Yield losses of ten to thirty percent are common in susceptible hybrids under favorable conditions, and losses exceeding fifty percent have been recorded in severe epidemics. The impact is greatest when the ear leaf and leaves above it become heavily infected before grain fill is complete. Early-season infection that reaches the upper canopy before tasseling is the most economically damaging scenario. The disease does not kill the corn plant outright but significantly reduces grain yield and test weight.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
The incubation period ranges from seven to twelve days depending on temperature and humidity. The disease typically appears first on lower leaves and progresses upward through the canopy as the season advances. The most critical period for yield impact is from silking through grain fill. Under favorable conditions of cool temperatures and high humidity, the disease can spread rapidly through a susceptible corn stand within two to three weeks of initial infection.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
Northern Leaf Blight overwinters in corn residue on the soil surface. In spring and early summer, conidia are produced from infected residue and are carried by wind to developing corn plants. Initial infections on lower leaves produce abundant conidia within the lesions that continue to spread upward through the canopy via wind dispersal. The pathogen can spread within a field and between fields over significant distances. Continuous corn production and no-till practices that leave residue on the soil surface increase inoculum levels and disease risk.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Exserohilum turcicum is a deuteromycete fungus that produces large, distinctive boat-shaped conidia with multiple septa. It overwinters primarily in infected corn residue, where it produces conidia during wet periods throughout the growing season. The fungus infects corn tissue by penetrating through stomata or directly through the cuticle. It requires free water on the leaf surface for spore germination and infection. Race variation within the pathogen population affects the effectiveness of host resistance genes in commercial hybrids.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On leaves, Northern Leaf Blight produces large, elliptical to cigar-shaped lesions that are three to fifteen centimeters long. Young lesions are gray-green and water-soaked in appearance. As they mature, lesions become tan to grayish-brown with wavy margins that are not restricted by leaf veins. Under humid conditions, dark gray-green sporulation is visible within the lesions, giving them a dirty appearance. In severe cases, multiple lesions coalesce and kill entire leaves, causing premature senescence of the canopy. On husks and leaf sheaths, lesions may also develop but are less common and less economically significant than foliar infection.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Northern Leaf Blight thrives in temperatures between eighteen and twenty-seven degrees Celsius, with optimum infection around twenty-two degrees. The fungus requires six to eighteen hours of leaf wetness for successful infection, depending on temperature. Cool nights, morning dews, and frequent light rains create ideal conditions. The disease is most severe in years with cool, cloudy, humid weather during the growing season and in fields with heavy corn residue from no-till or continuous corn production.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
The cycle begins with conidia produced from infected corn residue in spring. These spores are windborne and infect young corn plants, initially causing lesions on lower leaves. Sporulating lesions produce additional conidia that spread upward through the canopy. The disease progresses most rapidly during periods of cool, humid weather. At the end of the season, infected crop residue falls to the soil surface and serves as the primary inoculum source for the following season.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
Cultural management begins with crop rotation away from corn for one to two years, which allows infected residue to decompose and reduces primary inoculum. Tillage to incorporate residue also reduces surface inoculum but must be balanced with soil health considerations. Selecting resistant hybrids is the most cost-effective and sustainable management strategy and is strongly recommended for fields with a history of Northern Leaf Blight or continuous corn production. Hybrid selection is the foundation of management. Many commercial corn hybrids carry resistance genes designated Ht1, Ht2, Ht3, or HtN that provide race-specific resistance. Non-race-specific quantitative resistance is also available in some hybrids and provides more durable protection. Fields with a history of the disease should use hybrids with the highest available resistance ratings.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Fungicide applications are economically justified in susceptible hybrids when disease is present at or below the ear leaf at tasseling. The critical timing for fungicide application is at tasseling through silking, as this protects the ear leaf and upper canopy during grain fill. Fungicide classes used include triazoles such as propiconazole and tebuconazole, strobilurins such as azoxystrobin and pyraclostrobin, and premixed combinations of both classes. Single applications at tasseling are typically sufficient; a second application is rarely economically justified unless disease pressure is extreme.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
Before planting, select hybrids with Northern Leaf Blight resistance ratings appropriate for fields with residue from previous corn crops. During the growing season, begin scouting at V6 and assess disease progress at tasseling. When lesions are found below the ear leaf at tasseling in a susceptible hybrid, apply a foliar fungicide immediately. When lesions reach the ear leaf or above before grain fill is complete, consider economic losses already incurred and adjust management for the following season.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Scout fields weekly from V6 through silking. Examine leaves throughout the canopy, beginning with lower leaves where infection typically starts. Assess the vertical progress of the disease relative to the ear leaf and the growth stage of the crop. Fields with continuous corn, no-till, or a history of Northern Leaf Blight should be scouted most intensively. Make fungicide decisions at tasseling based on disease level at that timing.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Northern Leaf Blight is most severe in the upper Midwest of the United States, the corn belt of Europe, and humid corn-producing regions of South America and Africa. It is present wherever corn is grown in cool, humid conditions and is less damaging in hot, dry climates where leaf wetness periods are short.
