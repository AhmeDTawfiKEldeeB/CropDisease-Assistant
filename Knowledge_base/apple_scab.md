# Apple Scab - Apple

**Pathogen:** Venturia inaequalis  
**Type:** Fungal  
**Host:** Apple (Malus domestica)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Apple Scab is a fungal disease caused by Venturia inaequalis that attacks apple trees in cool, moist climates. It is one of the most economically damaging diseases in apple production worldwide, capable of causing severe defoliation and rendering fruit unmarketable within a single season if left unmanaged. The disease affects leaves, blossoms, and fruit, but its most characteristic and economically destructive effect is on the fruit, which develops dark, corky, scab-like lesions that reduce quality and marketability. It is widespread in temperate apple-growing regions and is considered the primary fungal threat to commercial apple production.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Apple Scab is rated as a very high danger disease. It does not kill the apple tree itself, but it can destroy the commercial value of one hundred percent of the fruit crop in a single season under cool, wet conditions. Defoliation in severe cases weakens the tree and reduces productivity in subsequent years. The speed of damage is moderate, with lesions developing over one to three weeks following infection. The economic impact is among the highest of any tree fruit disease worldwide, requiring intensive fungicide programs in susceptible regions.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
The incubation period ranges from nine to seventeen days depending on temperature, with the fastest development around twenty degrees Celsius. The most critical infection window is from green tip through petal fall, coinciding with primary ascospore release. Once a leaf or fruit is infected, visible symptoms appear within one to two weeks. The disease spreads fastest during cool, wet spring weather coinciding with early leaf development and bloom.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
Apple Scab overwinters in infected leaves on the orchard floor. In spring, rainfall triggers the release of ascospores from pseudothecia that develop in overwintered leaf debris. These spores are carried by wind and rain splash to emerging leaves, blossoms, and developing fruit. Young tissue is the most vulnerable and can be infected from green tip through several weeks after petal fall. After primary infections establish, the fungus produces secondary spores called conidia from new lesions, which continue spreading through the season via rain splash. Overwintered leaf debris on the orchard floor is the single most important source of primary inoculum.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Venturia inaequalis is an ascomycete fungus with two reproductive stages. The sexual stage produces ascospores inside pseudothecia that form in overwintered infected leaves. These ascospores are the primary inoculum released in spring during rainfall events. The asexual stage produces conidia from sporulating lesions on leaves and fruit during the growing season, providing secondary inoculum throughout the summer. The fungus penetrates host tissue through the cuticle and requires free water on the tissue surface for spore germination and infection. It does not persist in soil.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On leaves, Apple Scab produces olive-green to brown velvety spots on the upper surface that become darker and more defined with age. Heavily infected leaves may yellow and drop prematurely, leading to defoliation in severe cases. On fruit, small, circular, olive-brown lesions appear that become dark, corky, and raised as they age. Multiple lesions can merge to cover large areas of the fruit surface. Infected fruit may crack and become deformed as it grows. On blossoms and stems, dark lesions can appear on pedicels and cause flower drop before fruit set, directly reducing yield. On shoots, infected tissue may show dark lesions that can become perennial cankers in susceptible varieties.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Apple Scab thrives in temperatures between six and twenty-four degrees Celsius, with optimum infection occurring around eighteen to twenty degrees. The fungus requires continuous leaf wetness for spore germination and infection. The Mills Table is used to determine infection risk based on the combination of temperature and hours of leaf wetness. Cool, wet spring weather with frequent rain events is the primary driver of severe epidemics.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
The cycle begins with the fungus overwintering in infected leaves on the orchard floor. Spring rains trigger the release of ascospores, which infect young tissue during wet periods. After an incubation period of nine to seventeen days, lesions form on leaves and developing fruit. Conidia produced from these lesions provide secondary inoculum for continued spread throughout the growing season. Infected leaves fall to the orchard floor in autumn, completing the cycle and providing inoculum for the following spring.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
Cultural management begins with the removal or burial of infected leaf debris before spring to reduce primary inoculum. Pruning to improve canopy airflow reduces humidity and leaf wetness duration. Removing water sprouts and maintaining open canopy architecture is important for drying. Planting scab-resistant varieties eliminates the need for intensive fungicide programs. Variety selection is the most sustainable long-term strategy. Resistant varieties such as Liberty, Enterprise, and Crimson Crisp provide good to excellent resistance and dramatically reduce spray requirements. Most commercial varieties including Gala, Fuji, and Honeycrisp are highly susceptible and require full spray programs.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Chemical control should begin at green tip and continue through primary scab season, typically until two to three weeks after petal fall. Fungicide classes used include multi-site protectants such as captan and mancozeb, sterol-inhibiting triazoles such as myclobutanil and fenbuconazole, strobilurins such as trifloxystrobin, and SDHI products. Modes of action must be rotated to prevent fungicide resistance. Systemic fungicides can be used post-infection within seventy-two hours of a wetting event. In organic programs, sulfur and copper are the primary options and must be applied before infection events.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
Before budbreak, remove or shred fallen leaf debris to reduce primary inoculum. At green tip, begin protective fungicide applications. During the primary infection period from green tip through petal fall, use infection risk models to time sprays. When first lesions appear on leaves, switch to a systemic fungicide and increase monitoring frequency. After harvest, shred or remove fallen leaves before winter.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Scout weekly from green tip through midsummer. Focus inspection on the underside of leaves and developing fruit in the lower canopy where humidity is highest. Use weather-based infection models such as the Mills Table or RIMpro to guide spray timing. After wet periods during the primary infection window, inspect within seven to ten days for first lesions.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Apple Scab is most severe in humid temperate climates, particularly in the northeastern United States, northern Europe, and New Zealand. It causes less damage in dry climates such as the interior western United States where spring rainfall is minimal.
