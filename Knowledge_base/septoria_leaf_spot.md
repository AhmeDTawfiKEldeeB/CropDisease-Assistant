# Septoria Leaf Spot - Tomato

**Pathogen:** Septoria lycopersici  
**Type:** Fungal  
**Host:** Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Septoria Leaf Spot is a fungal disease of tomato caused by Septoria lycopersici and is one of the most common tomato diseases in humid regions worldwide. It produces numerous small circular spots with gray centers and dark brown borders, and within each spot, tiny black dots are visible to the naked eye or under a hand lens. These black dots are pycnidia, the flask-shaped fruiting bodies of the fungus that release spores only when wet, and they are the definitive diagnostic feature that distinguishes Septoria from all other tomato leaf spot diseases. The disease begins on the oldest lower leaves after fruit set and moves steadily upward through the canopy. It rarely damages fruit directly, but severe defoliation exposes fruits to sunscald and weakens the plant's ability to fill them properly. Rain splash is the essential driver of spread, and keeping foliage dry through drip irrigation and mulching is highly effective at slowing the disease.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Septoria Leaf Spot is rated as moderate to high danger. It rarely kills plants but heavy defoliation causes significant indirect damage through sunscald on exposed fruit, reduced photosynthesis, and weakened fruit fill. Yield losses in severe, unmanaged cases range from twenty to forty percent. The disease is most damaging when it progresses rapidly into the upper canopy before or during fruit fill. It is consistently among the most common reasons for tomato defoliation in the eastern United States and other humid regions.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
The incubation period from infection to visible symptoms is five to ten days. Progression through the canopy from lower to upper leaves occurs over four to eight weeks in typical seasons. The disease usually appears after fruit set and intensifies through harvest. The speed of progression is moderate compared to diseases like Late Blight or Bacterial Spot, but it is steady and cumulative without management intervention.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
Septoria Leaf Spot overwinters in infected plant debris in the soil as pycnidia, which remain viable for two or more seasons. When rain or overhead irrigation wets the debris, pycnidia release spores that are splashed upward from the soil surface onto lower leaves. This rain splash mechanism is the primary driver of spread and the reason why mulching and drip irrigation are so effective at slowing the disease. Spores that land on leaves penetrate through stomata on the leaf underside during wet periods. Within the new lesions, pycnidia form rapidly and the cycle repeats with each rain event, progressively splashing spores higher into the canopy. Workers and equipment moving through wet fields spread spores laterally between plants and between fields. Infected transplants can also introduce the disease into clean fields.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Septoria lycopersici is an ascomycete fungus that survives as pycnidia in infected plant debris in soil. The pycnidia are flask-shaped structures embedded in lesion tissue that release spores called pycnidiospores, which are long, filiform, and multiseptate. These spores are released only when the pycnidia are wetted by rain or irrigation, which is why the activation of spread is directly tied to rain events. The sexual stage of the fungus is called Mycosphaerella lycopersici but is rarely observed in practice. The pathogen is primarily a tomato pathogen but is also reported on eggplant, pepino, and some solanaceous weeds. It does not affect wheat or other cereals despite sharing the Septoria genus name with wheat pathogens, which are entirely unrelated organisms.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On leaves, Septoria Leaf Spot produces numerous small, circular to slightly irregular spots between one and four millimeters in diameter. Each spot has a dark brown border surrounding a lighter gray or tan center. Inside the center of each spot, tiny black dots are visible. These are the pycnidia and are the definitive identification feature of Septoria Leaf Spot. The disease begins exclusively on lower, older leaves and moves upward through the canopy progressively over the season. In severe cases, spots coalesce into large irregular blighted areas and leaves turn yellow and drop. Defoliation can be complete on the lower and middle canopy in unmanaged crops. On stems and petioles, similar small spots with dark borders can form. Stem infection is less common but possible in wet conditions. Petiole lesions may cause premature leaf drop. On fruit, small spots with the same appearance as leaf lesions can rarely occur on green tomatoes. Fruit infection is uncommon and is not the main economic concern. The primary fruit-related problem is sunscald on exposed fruits after defoliation removes leaf cover.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Septoria Leaf Spot thrives in temperatures between twenty and twenty-five degrees Celsius. Extended wet periods with rain or overhead irrigation are essential for the splash-dispersal mechanism that drives spread. The disease is most severe after fruit set when plant tissue is aging and the canopy is dense. Overhead irrigation in warm weather produces ideal conditions for rapid progression through the canopy.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
The cycle begins with pycnidia in infected plant debris overwintering in the soil. When rain or irrigation wets the debris, pycnidia release spores that are splashed onto lower leaves. Spores penetrate through stomata during wet periods. After five to ten days, new lesions form with new pycnidia inside them. Repeated rain events splash spores progressively higher into the canopy. Workers and tools spread spores laterally between plants. At season end, infected debris falls to the soil and pycnidia persist there for one to two seasons, ready to initiate the next season's epidemic.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
Crop rotation away from tomato, eggplant, and solanaceous hosts for two or more seasons is the most effective long-term cultural tool because it allows pycnidia in the soil debris to degrade and substantially reduces the inoculum load for subsequent tomato crops. Organic mulch applied around the base of plants physically prevents rain splash from soil to lower leaves and is one of the most cost-effective early-season preventive measures available. Switching from overhead to drip irrigation eliminates the main driver of spore splash within the field and can dramatically slow disease progression on its own. Removing and destroying infected lower leaves as the disease progresses reduces the inoculum load in the canopy and improves airflow. Staking and training plants improves airflow through the canopy. Workers should avoid moving through fields when foliage is wet. All plant debris should be thoroughly removed and composted or buried after harvest.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Fungicide applications should begin when first symptoms appear or when weather consistently favors infection. Multi-site protectants including chlorothalonil and mancozeb provide no curative activity and must be applied preventively every seven to ten days. Copper-based products are effective and approved for organic use. Strobilurins such as azoxystrobin and pyraclostrobin provide protectant and some curative activity. Triazoles such as difenoconazole and propiconazole are systemic with curative activity within forty-eight to seventy-two hours of infection. SDHI products such as boscalid can be used in premix formulations. Applications every seven to ten days are typical, increasing to every five to seven days during wet weather. Thorough coverage of lower canopy leaves is important because this is where the disease initiates. Modes of action should be rotated with every two applications because strobilurin resistance in Septoria populations has been reported in some regions. In organic programs, copper-based sprays are the most reliable option. Bacillus subtilis products have some suppressive activity and can support an integrated program. Mulching and drip irrigation remain the most impactful organic tools.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
When spots appear on only the lowest two to three leaves, remove the affected leaves, apply mulch, and begin a fungicide spray program. When disease is progressing into the mid-canopy near the fruit zone, spray a systemic fungicide immediately and stop overhead irrigation. When significant defoliation has occurred with fruit exposed to direct sun, apply fungicide and consider shade cloth for sun protection of fruit. When overhead irrigation is currently in use, switch to drip immediately because this single change can dramatically slow disease spread.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Begin scouting at fruit set because the disease rarely appears before that stage. Focus on lower canopy leaves first because the disease always begins at the plant base. Use a hand lens at ten times magnification to confirm the tiny black pycnidia in lesion centers, which is the definitive identification feature. Record the percentage of plants affected, the highest leaf position showing disease, and the percentage of defoliation. Track disease progress weekly. Rapid upward movement signals the need for immediate intervention. Weather monitoring for periods of three or more consecutive days of temperatures between twenty and twenty-five degrees with rain represents high-risk windows requiring increased vigilance.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Septoria Leaf Spot is present wherever tomatoes are grown in humid conditions. It is most severe in the eastern United States, northern and eastern Europe, parts of Latin America and the Caribbean, and South and Southeast Asia. It is less common in arid or semi-arid climates with little summer rainfall.
