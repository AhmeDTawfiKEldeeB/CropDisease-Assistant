# Leaf Mold - Tomato

**Pathogen:** Passalora fulva  
**Type:** Fungal  
**Host:** Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Leaf Mold is a fungal disease of tomato caused by Passalora fulva, formerly known as Cladosporium fulvum and also referred to as Fulvia fulva in some literature. It is primarily a disease of protected tomato production, most commonly occurring in greenhouses and high tunnels where humidity can rise and remain above the critical threshold of eighty-five percent relative humidity. Under those conditions the disease spreads rapidly and can strip all leaves from greenhouse plants within two to four weeks. In open-field production it is less common but can occur in dense plantings during prolonged wet and cloudy weather. The defining characteristic of the disease is an olive-green to grayish-purple velvety mold that forms on the underside of infected leaves, directly beneath pale yellow spots visible on the upper surface.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Leaf Mold is rated as moderate danger in open fields and high danger in greenhouses and high tunnels. It does not kill plants directly, but complete defoliation severely weakens them and eliminates yield by removing the photosynthetic surface needed to fill fruit. Fruit infection is rare, making defoliation the primary economic concern. The disease is one of the leading causes of losses in protected tomato production worldwide and requires consistent humidity management to keep under control.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
The incubation period from infection to visible symptoms is ten to fourteen days. In a greenhouse where humidity is consistently above eighty-five percent and ventilation is poor, the disease can spread through the entire structure in two to four weeks. In open-field conditions the spread is slower and more dependent on weather events. An important characteristic of the pathogen is that its conidia can survive on greenhouse structures including frames, plastic covering, stakes, and trellises for months to years between crop cycles, making them a persistent inoculum source for every new crop planted into an inadequately sanitized structure.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
The primary source in greenhouse production is infected plant debris and contaminated greenhouse surfaces where conidia survive between crops. At the start of each new crop cycle, humidity rises and dormant spores on structures become active and are carried by internal air currents from plant to plant. Workers handling infected plants transfer conidia on their hands, clothing, and tools, which is a significant pathway of spread especially in the early stages of an outbreak before the problem is recognized. Conidia germinate on the leaf underside and penetrate through stomata. The critical requirement is relative humidity above eighty-five percent. Below this threshold, conidia cannot germinate and the disease stops spreading regardless of how much inoculum is present. This single environmental threshold is the most important principle in managing Leaf Mold.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Passalora fulva is an ascomycete fungus with high race diversity, with over thirty races identified based on their ability to overcome specific resistance genes in tomato called Cf genes. The sexual stage is rarely observed, and the disease is propagated almost entirely through asexual conidia. These conidia are produced in chains on conidiophores that emerge from stomata on the leaf underside, creating the visible velvety sporulation. The spores are highly resilient and remain viable on surfaces including metal, plastic, and wood for extended periods between crops. The fungus requires relative humidity above eighty-five percent both for spore germination and for new sporulation, which is why humidity control is simultaneously the most important monitoring parameter and the most effective management tool.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On the upper leaf surface, Leaf Mold produces pale green to yellow spots of variable size with diffuse, irregular margins. These spots may be confused with nutrient deficiencies or other diseases unless the leaf underside is examined. On the lower leaf surface, directly beneath the upper surface spots, olive-green to grayish-purple velvety mold appears. This sporulation is the defining diagnostic feature of Leaf Mold. The color can range from olive-green early in the season to gray-brown as the season progresses, and can appear purplish under some conditions. This growth is the conidiophore production generating new infectious spores. Infected leaves curl at the edges, turn brown, and drop progressively from the lower to the upper canopy. In severe outbreaks, complete defoliation occurs within weeks. Stem and fruit infection are rare and only occur in very severe cases under extremely high humidity.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Relative humidity above eighty-five percent is the critical condition for Leaf Mold, both for spore germination and for new sporulation. Temperature between sixteen and twenty-four degrees Celsius, with an optimum around twenty-two degrees, is the most favorable range. Poor air circulation within a greenhouse creates local pockets of very high humidity that enable rapid spread even when average greenhouse humidity is somewhat lower. Overhead or foliar irrigation contributes directly to humidity and leaf wetness and accelerates the disease significantly.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
Conidia survive on greenhouse structures, in plant debris, and in soil between crop cycles. When a new crop is planted and canopy density increases, humidity rises above eighty-five percent and dormant conidia are activated. Air currents distribute spores through the greenhouse to leaf undersides. Conidia germinate and penetrate stomata during humid periods. After ten to fourteen days, new velvety sporulation appears on infected leaves and produces fresh conidia that air currents spread further. The cycle repeats rapidly as long as humidity remains above the threshold. At season end, conidia remain on greenhouse surfaces and in debris to initiate the cycle in the next crop.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
Reducing relative humidity below eighty-five percent is the most important and most effective management action. This can be achieved by opening vents and running circulation fans, heating the greenhouse in the morning to reduce overnight condensation, reducing irrigation frequency, and avoiding all overhead or foliar watering. Below eighty-five percent relative humidity, sporulation stops and the disease ceases to spread regardless of inoculum levels, making environmental control more powerful than any chemical option. Plant spacing should be adequate to allow airflow through the canopy. Infected leaves should be removed promptly and disposed of outside the greenhouse rather than composted inside. Workers should implement hygiene protocols including changing clothes and washing hands before entering high-value or clean sections. Between crop cycles, thorough sanitization of the entire greenhouse is essential. All plant material must be completely removed. Surfaces including frames, plastic, stakes, trellises, and irrigation equipment should be washed with detergent to remove organic matter, then treated with bleach at one to ten dilution or quaternary ammonium compounds, and allowed to dry completely for at least one week before the next crop is transplanted. Resistant varieties carrying Cf resistance genes are available and provide effective protection against specific races of the pathogen. However, resistance is race-specific and breaks down when a new race matching the variety's Cf gene is present. Combining resistant varieties with humidity management provides the most reliable protection.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Fungicide applications work significantly better when combined with humidity reduction and should not be relied upon as a substitute for environmental control. Effective fungicide classes include multi-site protectants such as chlorothalonil and mancozeb, strobilurins such as azoxystrobin, and triazoles such as difenoconazole. Applications every seven to ten days with thorough coverage of leaf undersides are required in active disease situations. Strobilurin resistance has been reported in some greenhouse populations, so modes of action should be rotated. In organic programs, copper-based fungicides and Bacillus subtilis products provide some suppressive activity. Humidity management and thorough greenhouse sanitization between crops are the primary organic tools.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
When humidity is consistently above eighty-five percent, open all vents, run circulation fans, and reduce irrigation frequency immediately because this is the priority action before any chemical response. When first olive mold appears on leaf undersides, remove infected leaves immediately, spray fungicide with thorough underside coverage, and urgently increase ventilation. When disease is spreading rapidly through a greenhouse, combine emergency ventilation with plant removal in the worst areas and fungicide application throughout. Between crops, sanitize all greenhouse structures, stakes, and equipment before the next planting.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Check plants two to three times per week in greenhouses throughout the growing season. Use a hygrometer to monitor humidity continuously and flag any periods when it consistently exceeds eighty-five percent. Scout during morning hours when sporulation is most visible. Check leaf undersides first because olive mold appears before upper surface yellowing becomes obvious. Record the percentage of plants affected and the severity of sporulation to track disease progression and the effectiveness of ventilation interventions.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Leaf Mold is primarily a disease of protected tomato production and is most economically significant in major greenhouse tomato regions including the Netherlands, the United Kingdom, Scandinavia, Spain, Italy, Turkey, Japan, South Korea, and greenhouse production areas of North America. Open-field occurrence is limited to warm, humid coastal environments where sustained humidity above eighty-five percent is possible.
