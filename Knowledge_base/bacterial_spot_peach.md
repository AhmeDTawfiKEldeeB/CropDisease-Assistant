# Bacterial Spot - Peach

**Pathogen:** Xanthomonas arboricola pv. pruni  
**Type:** Bacterial  
**Host:** Peach (Prunus persica)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Bacterial Spot is a bacterial disease caused by Xanthomonas arboricola pv. pruni that attacks peach trees in warm, humid climates. It is the most common and economically important bacterial disease of peach and nectarine worldwide, causing lesions on leaves, fruit, and twigs. The disease is particularly damaging on fruit, where lesions reduce marketability and create entry points for secondary fungal pathogens. Severe defoliation caused by the disease weakens trees and increases susceptibility to winter injury. It is present in all major peach-producing regions and is most severe in the eastern United States where warm, rainy springs and summers are common.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Bacterial Spot is rated as a high danger disease on peach. In susceptible varieties during wet seasons, fruit losses of fifty to one hundred percent have been documented. Fruit with lesions cannot be marketed fresh and are often downgraded or rejected for processing as well. Repeated severe defoliation weakens tree structure, reduces cold hardiness, and shortens productive orchard life. The combination of fruit loss, defoliation, and long-term tree decline makes this one of the most economically important diseases in peach production.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
The incubation period is three to five days under warm conditions, which allows for multiple infection cycles within a single growing season. The disease can spread rapidly during warm, rainy periods. The most critical infection windows are during petal fall and early fruit development for fruit infections, and throughout the growing season for foliar infections. Twig infections that occur in late summer provide overwintering inoculum for the following season.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
Xanthomonas arboricola pv. pruni overwinters in infected twig tissue and cankers formed on current-season growth. In spring, bacteria ooze from infected twigs during wet weather and are spread by wind-driven rain to leaves and developing fruit. Secondary spread from leaf to leaf and from leaf to fruit occurs throughout the season during rain events. The bacterium enters host tissue through natural openings such as stomata and hydathodes and through wounds caused by insects, hail, or blowing sand. High winds that drive rain into open stomata or create micro-wounds are particularly effective in promoting infection.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Xanthomonas arboricola pv. pruni is a gram-negative, rod-shaped bacterium that multiplies in intercellular spaces of host tissue. It produces enzymes that break down cell walls and cause the water-soaked lesions characteristic of early infection. The bacterium can survive for extended periods in infected twig tissue and spreads through water films on plant surfaces. It has a wide host range within stone fruits including plum, cherry, and apricot, though peach is among the most susceptible hosts.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On leaves, Bacterial Spot causes small, water-soaked angular spots that are initially yellow-green and become brown to purple-brown with age. Lesions are typically angular because they are restricted by leaf veins. The center of older lesions often falls out, giving the leaf a shot-hole appearance. Severely infected leaves turn yellow and drop, causing premature defoliation. On fruit, small, water-soaked spots develop that become sunken, brown, and corky as they age. Lesions can reach several centimeters in diameter and may crack, creating entry points for secondary infections. Severe fruit infections cause deep pitting and cracking that renders the fruit unmarketable. On twigs, the bacterium causes dark, water-soaked spots in late summer that develop into spring cankers. These cankers are the primary overwintering site and source of primary inoculum in spring.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Bacterial Spot thrives in temperatures between twenty-four and thirty degrees Celsius, with infection possible across a range of fifteen to thirty-five degrees. The bacterium requires free water on tissue surfaces for spread and infection. Wind-driven rain is particularly effective in promoting infection. Sandy soils that cause blowing sand abrasion increase wound sites available for infection. High nitrogen fertility that promotes succulent, susceptible growth increases disease severity.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
The cycle begins with bacteria overwintering in infected twig tissue. In spring, bacteria ooze from infected twigs during wet weather and are spread by rain to developing leaves and fruit. Multiple infection cycles occur throughout the season during rain events. In late summer, new twig infections establish the overwintering inoculum for the following season. The disease is self-sustaining within an orchard once established unless twig infections are managed.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
Cultural management includes selecting resistant varieties where possible, pruning out infected twig tissue during dormancy, and avoiding practices that increase tree stress and susceptibility. Avoiding excessive nitrogen fertilization reduces the production of highly susceptible succulent growth. Windbreaks that reduce wind-driven rain penetration into the orchard can reduce infection rates. Drip irrigation is preferred over overhead systems to reduce leaf wetness. Variety selection is the most powerful tool available. Highly susceptible varieties such as Redhaven and Reliance suffer severe losses in wet seasons, while varieties with good resistance such as Contender and Biscoe show significantly lower damage levels.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Chemical control relies primarily on copper-based bactericides applied from petal fall through cover sprays during the growing season. Copper applications must begin at shuck split and be maintained on a seven to ten day interval during wet weather. The number of applications required varies by season and variety susceptibility. Resistance to copper has developed in some Xanthomonas populations, so resistance management is important where repeated copper use has occurred. Oxytetracycline is also registered for use on peach in some regions and provides an alternative mode of action. No fungicide has activity against the bacterial pathogen.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
Before bloom, prune out all visible infected twig tissue with dark cankers and destroy it. At shuck split, begin copper bactericide applications and do not allow gaps during wet periods. When first leaf lesions appear, increase spray frequency to every five to seven days during wet weather. When fruit lesions are detected, assess severity and consider economic impact on marketability. After harvest, evaluate twig infection levels and schedule dormant pruning for infected material.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Scout trees weekly from petal fall through harvest. Examine the inner canopy and lower leaves where disease is typically found first. During and after rain events, increase inspection frequency and assess new lesion development. At harvest, assess the percentage of fruit with lesions to determine economic impact and plan management for the following season. During dormant pruning, assess the density of infected twigs as an indicator of inoculum pressure.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Bacterial Spot of peach is most severe in the eastern United States, where warm, rainy summers are common. It is present in all major peach-producing regions worldwide including Europe, South America, and Asia. The disease is less damaging in dry climates such as California where summer rainfall is minimal.
