# Black Rot - Apple

**Pathogen:** Botryosphaeria obtusa  
**Type:** Fungal  
**Host:** Apple (Malus domestica)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Black Rot is a fungal disease caused by Botryosphaeria obtusa that attacks apple trees in warm, humid climates. It is a significant disease in apple production, causing fruit rot, leaf spot, and cankers on branches. The disease affects leaves, fruit, and woody tissue, but its most economically damaging effect is on the fruit, which develops a characteristic rotting pattern with concentric rings of brown and black that eventually consumes the entire fruit. Infected fruit shrivel into black mummified forms that remain on the tree. It is widespread in humid apple-growing regions and is capable of causing severe losses in unmanaged or stressed orchards.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Black Rot of apple is rated as a high danger disease. Unlike its grape counterpart, it rarely destroys an entire crop in one season under normal conditions, but it can cause significant fruit losses and persistent canker problems that weaken or kill limbs over multiple seasons. Fruit losses of thirty to fifty percent have been recorded in neglected orchards. The combination of fruit rot, foliar disease, and wood cankers makes it a multifaceted threat that can compound over time if not managed.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
The incubation period on fruit ranges from one to three weeks depending on temperature and fruit maturity. The most critical infection window for fruit is from petal fall through the summer growing season, with late-season infections often going undetected until storage. Canker infections can occur throughout the growing season whenever wounded or stressed tissue is present. The disease spreads fastest during warm, wet summer weather.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
Black Rot overwinters in mummified fruit, cankers on branches, and infected wood debris. In spring and throughout the growing season, rainfall triggers the release of conidia and ascospores from these sources. Spores are spread by wind and rain splash to leaves, fruit, and wounded wood. Fruit infections typically enter through wounds, lenticels, or the calyx end. The fungus is also an opportunistic pathogen that readily infects trees stressed by winter injury, fire blight, or mechanical damage. Mummified fruit left on the tree or on the ground are important sources of inoculum.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Botryosphaeria obtusa is an ascomycete fungus that produces both sexual ascospores and asexual conidia. Fruiting bodies form in overwintered canker tissue and mummified fruit and release spores during wet weather throughout the growing season. The fungus can persist for multiple years in canker tissue on living branches, making it a chronic source of inoculum within the orchard. It is also a weak pathogen that readily infects stressed or wounded tissue but can infect healthy fruit under favorable conditions.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On fruit, Black Rot begins as a small brown spot that expands rapidly into a firm rot with characteristic concentric rings of alternating brown and black. Eventually the entire fruit turns black and shrinks into a mummified form. Pycnidia are visible as small black dots on the fruit surface. On leaves, the disease causes frog-eye leaf spot, circular spots with purple margins and tan or brown centers. These spots are characteristic and help distinguish the disease from other leaf diseases. On branches, the fungus causes oval to elongated cankers that are brown or reddish-brown and may ooze during wet weather. Cankers enlarge slowly and can girdle and kill limbs over several years.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Black Rot thrives in temperatures between twenty-four and thirty degrees Celsius, with infection occurring across a wide range. Warm, wet summer weather favors rapid fruit rot development. Trees stressed by drought, winter injury, insect damage, or fire blight are significantly more susceptible. Poorly pruned orchards with abundant dead wood provide ideal overwintering habitat for the pathogen.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
The cycle begins with the fungus overwintering in mummified fruit, cankers, and dead wood. Spores are released during wet weather from spring through the end of the growing season. Leaves are infected early in the season, and fruit infections occur from petal fall onward. Infected fruit develop visible rot symptoms within one to three weeks and eventually mummify. Canker infections expand slowly throughout the season and continue in subsequent years.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
Cultural management is central to control. All mummified fruit must be removed from trees and the orchard floor before spring. Dead wood, cankers, and fire blight strikes should be pruned out and destroyed. Wounds from pruning or storm damage should be minimized and made during dry weather when possible. Maintaining tree health through proper nutrition and irrigation reduces susceptibility. Canopy pruning to improve airflow and light penetration reduces humidity and accelerates drying.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Chemical control focuses on protecting fruit from petal fall through harvest. Fungicide classes used include captan as a broad-spectrum protectant, triazoles such as myclobutanil and tebuconazole, and strobilurins. Applications during summer should be timed to cover periods of warm, wet weather. Fungicide rotation is important to prevent resistance. Canker control relies primarily on cultural practices, as fungicides do not cure established canker infections.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
Before budbreak, remove all mummies and dead wood from trees and the orchard floor. At petal fall, begin protective fungicide applications. When frog-eye leaf spots appear, assess canker severity and schedule pruning. When fruit rot is found, remove infected fruit immediately and apply a systemic fungicide. After harvest, remove remaining mummies and conduct thorough dormant pruning of cankers.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Scout weekly from petal fall through harvest. Examine fruit carefully in the lower and inner canopy where disease is often first detected. Assess canker presence on limbs during dormant pruning. After harvest, survey mummy levels on trees and on the ground to gauge inoculum pressure for the following season.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Black Rot of apple is most severe in the eastern United States, where warm, humid summers favor disease development. It is present in apple-growing regions worldwide but is less damaging in dry climates where summer rainfall is limited.
