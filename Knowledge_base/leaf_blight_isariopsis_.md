# Isariopsis Leaf Spot - Grape
Pathogen: Pseudocercospora vitis | Type: Fungal | Host: Grapevine (Vitis spp.)


## Definition

Isariopsis Leaf Spot, also known as Grape Leaf Blight, is a fungal disease of grapevines caused by Pseudocercospora vitis, previously known under the synonyms Isariopsis clavispora and Cercospora viticola. The disease primarily affects leaves, producing dark brown spots with a characteristic dark olive-colored sporulation on the underside that is its most reliable diagnostic feature. It causes progressive leaf yellowing and premature defoliation, weakening the vine as it enters winter dormancy. Isariopsis Leaf Spot is less aggressive and less economically important than Black Rot or Downy Mildew, but it can cause significant defoliation in warm, humid regions, particularly in tropical and subtropical grape-growing areas, and its impact accumulates over multiple seasons when management is neglected.


## Severity and Danger Level

Isariopsis Leaf Spot is rated as moderate danger. It does not kill the grapevine and rarely causes direct fruit loss because berry infection is uncommon. Its primary harm is through defoliation, which reduces the vine's ability to produce and store carbohydrates before winter dormancy. Vines repeatedly defoliated by this disease in late summer and autumn enter winter with reduced energy reserves, lower cold hardiness, and diminished vigor for the following season. The economic impact is lower than Black Rot or Downy Mildew but meaningful in tropical and subtropical regions where the disease is most prevalent.


## Time to Spread

The incubation period from infection to visible symptoms is fourteen to twenty-one days. The disease builds slowly through summer, reaching its peak intensity in late summer and autumn. Significant defoliation, when it occurs, develops over four to eight weeks. The progression is gradual compared to more aggressive diseases, which means that well-timed management interventions can effectively control the season's impact.


## How It Spreads

Isariopsis Leaf Spot overwinters in infected fallen leaves on the vineyard floor, where the fungus survives as mycelium and conidia in the debris. In spring and early summer, warming temperatures and wet conditions stimulate spore production on this overwintered debris. Conidia are dispersed by wind and rain splash to the undersides of leaves, where they penetrate through stomata during periods of high humidity. Secondary infections occur throughout the growing season as new spore production from established lesions continues. The disease does not spread through soil, through root contact, or through pruning tools, unlike Esca. Infected leaves falling to the vineyard floor at the end of the season replenish the inoculum source for the following year.


## Pathogen Biology

Pseudocercospora vitis is an ascomycete fungus that survives as mycelium and conidia in infected fallen leaf debris on the vineyard floor. It produces long, multiseptate conidia in olive-colored clusters called fascicles on the underside of infected leaves. The conidia are produced most abundantly during periods of high relative humidity and do not require rain for their formation, only humid air. Penetration occurs exclusively through stomata on the leaf underside. The fungus does not persist long-term in soil and is entirely dependent on leaf litter for overwintering. This dependency on leaf debris makes litter removal the most powerful single management intervention available.


## Symptoms

On leaves, Isariopsis Leaf Spot produces irregular, angular to rounded spots between two and fifteen millimeters in diameter. On the upper leaf surface the spots appear dark brown to reddish-brown with diffuse, sometimes unclear margins. On the lower leaf surface, directly beneath the spots, dark olivaceous sporulation appears, ranging from olive-green to black in color. This dark olive underside growth is the key diagnostic feature that distinguishes this disease from Downy Mildew, which produces white sporulation on the leaf underside. Affected lesions may coalesce into large blighted areas. Infected leaves turn yellow and drop prematurely, and defoliation can be substantial in severe cases during late summer and autumn.

On petioles, dark elongated lesions can appear, occasionally causing premature leaf drop before the leaf itself shows complete symptoms.

On berries, small dark spots occasionally form on the berry surface near harvest, but berry infection is uncommon and rarely causes significant direct fruit loss.


## Favorable Conditions

Isariopsis Leaf Spot thrives in temperatures between twenty and thirty degrees Celsius and under conditions of high relative humidity with frequent rainfall. Dense canopies with poor air circulation create the microclimate most favorable to the disease by trapping humidity near the leaf surface. The disease is most severe in late summer and autumn and is most prevalent in tropical and subtropical grape-growing regions where humidity remains high throughout the season.


## Disease Cycle

The fungus survives winter in infected leaf debris on the vineyard floor. When warm, moist conditions return in spring and early summer, the pathogen produces conidia on the debris that are dispersed by wind and rain splash to leaf undersides. Infection occurs through stomata during humid periods. After an incubation of fourteen to twenty-one days, symptoms develop and new conidia are produced on the underside of infected leaves. Secondary infections continue throughout the growing season. As the season advances into late summer and autumn, the disease intensifies and defoliation accelerates. Infected leaves fall to the ground and replenish the leaf litter inoculum pool for the following season.


## Treatment

Removing and destroying fallen infected leaves after harvest is the single most effective management action for Isariopsis Leaf Spot. Because the disease depends entirely on leaf litter for overwintering, thorough leaf removal from the vineyard floor eliminates the primary inoculum source and directly reduces next season's disease pressure.

Improving canopy airflow through shoot positioning, leaf removal, and appropriate training systems reduces the humidity microclimate that favors the disease. Drip or subsurface irrigation should be used in preference to overhead systems. Balanced fertilization with avoidance of excess nitrogen keeps canopy density manageable.

## Management

This disease is usually managed as a secondary benefit of fungicide programs targeting the more serious diseases of Black Rot and Downy Mildew, because the fungicides used for those diseases, including copper, mancozeb, captan, azoxystrobin, and tebuconazole, are also effective against Pseudocercospora vitis. When Isariopsis Leaf Spot requires a dedicated chemical program, applications of copper or mancozeb every ten to fourteen days during wet periods provide adequate protection. Strobilurins and triazoles can be added when additional systemic activity is needed. Modes of action should be rotated to prevent resistance.

In organic programs, copper-based fungicides applied preventively are the most reliable option. The emphasis should be on leaf litter removal and canopy management as the primary organic tools.


## Quick Actions

In early summer when no symptoms are yet visible, remove leaf litter from the previous season, improve canopy airflow, and apply a preventive copper spray if wet weather is forecast. When first spots appear on lower leaves, spray fungicide and remove heavily infected leaves. When significant defoliation is occurring, spray immediately and remove fallen leaves from the vineyard floor to reduce active inoculum. After harvest, collect and destroy all fallen leaves, because this is the intervention that most directly reduces the following year's disease pressure.


## Scouting and Monitoring

Begin scouting in mid-summer when the disease typically starts to appear. Focus on lower canopy leaves where the disease initiates. Examine leaf undersides with a hand lens, looking for dark olive-colored conidiophore clusters that confirm Pseudocercospora vitis rather than another disease. Record the percentage of leaves with symptoms and estimate the percentage defoliation occurring before harvest. Monitor more intensively in humid, low-lying areas of the vineyard where air drainage is poorest.


## Geographic Distribution

Isariopsis Leaf Spot occurs worldwide wherever grapevines are grown, but is most economically significant in Southeast Asia, South America, and parts of southern and eastern Europe. It is particularly prevalent in India, China, Japan, Brazil, and Argentina. In dry Mediterranean climates it causes less pressure, but warm, wet years can produce significant outbreaks even in drier regions.
