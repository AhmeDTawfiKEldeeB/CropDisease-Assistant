# Downy Mildew - Grape
Pathogen: Plasmopara viticola | Type: Oomycete (Water Mold) | Host: Grapevine (Vitis spp.)


## Definition

Grape Downy Mildew is one of the most destructive diseases in viticulture worldwide, caused by Plasmopara viticola, an oomycete or water mold rather than a true fungus. This distinction is critical because it means standard fungicides effective against true fungi do not work against this pathogen, and only products specifically labeled for oomycete diseases can provide control. The disease creates pale yellow oil spots on the upper surface of leaves and a characteristic white, downy growth on the underside. It attacks leaves, shoots, tendrils, and fruit clusters with devastating speed during warm, rainy weather. A single warm, wet night can initiate a massive infection event across an entire vineyard. European wine grape varieties are extremely susceptible, while American varieties carry natural partial resistance. Plasmopara viticola originated in North America and was accidentally introduced to Europe in the 1870s, nearly destroying the European wine industry before control measures were developed.


## Severity and Danger Level

Grape Downy Mildew is rated as very high danger. Repeated severe defoliation weakens vines significantly, and young vines can be killed outright in their first season. Yield losses in unprotected susceptible varieties during wet seasons commonly reach fifty to one hundred percent. The economic impact is catastrophic when cluster infection occurs, because infected grapes are unmarketable and produce off-flavors in wine. The disease spreads so rapidly that by the time oil spots are visible, the vineyard may already be heavily infected beyond what can be seen. Once established, systemic fungicides provide only limited curative activity of twenty-four to seventy-two hours, making preventive protection essential.


## Time to Spread

The incubation period ranges from five to eighteen days, with shorter periods in warm weather around twenty degrees Celsius and longer periods in cool conditions. During a rain event, sporangia released from sporulating lesions disperse to the entire vineyard within hours through wind and rain. From the first oil spots to complete canopy infection takes only one to three weeks in warm, wet conditions. This makes Downy Mildew one of the fastest-spreading diseases in all of viticulture.


## How It Spreads

The primary overwintering source is oospores, which are sexually produced thick-walled spores that survive in fallen infected leaves and in the soil. In spring, oospores germinate after rain and release zoospores that swim through water films on leaf surfaces to stomata, where they initiate primary infections. The trigger point for primary infection risk is captured in the ten-ten-ten rule: the disease begins when shoots reach ten centimeters, air temperature exceeds ten degrees Celsius, and ten millimeters of rain have fallen in the previous twenty-four hours. Once primary infections establish, the white downy growth on leaf undersides produces sporangia that contain more zoospores. Rain and wind disperse these sporangia across the entire vineyard. Cluster infection is most devastating from flowering through berry set, when infected flowers and young berries turn brown and die. Long-distance introduction of the pathogen occurs through infected planting material brought to new vineyards.


## Pathogen Biology

Plasmopara viticola is an oomycete, a group of organisms more closely related to algae and brown algae than to true fungi. This evolutionary distance explains why fungicides that work by targeting fungal-specific biochemical pathways are ineffective against it. The pathogen produces two types of asexual spores: macrosporangia that are dispersed by wind and rain, and zoospores released from the macrosporangia in the presence of water that swim through water films to reach stomata. The sexual stage produces oospores through the mating of compatible strains, and these oospores provide the long-term survival in soil across seasons. The pathogen has shown the ability to evolve resistance to phenylamide fungicides such as metalaxyl, and this resistance is now widespread globally.


## Symptoms

On the upper leaf surface, Downy Mildew produces pale yellow to lime-green oil spots that appear translucent and greasy when held to light. These spots are roughly circular but are sometimes limited by leaf veins into angular shapes. Multiple spots coalesce to cover large areas of the leaf, which eventually turn brown and die.

On the lower leaf surface, directly beneath the oil spots, a white, cottony, fluffy growth appears. This is the sporangial growth, the production of infectious spore packets, and it is the primary diagnostic sign of active Downy Mildew. The color of this growth can range from pure white in early season infections to grayish or brownish as oospores form later in the season.

On shoots and tendrils, brown to black lesions form and shoot tips may die and curl into a shepherd's crook shape. Infected tendrils fail to grip and lose their structural function.

On clusters, infected flower clusters turn brown and die before they can set fruit, which is the most devastating outcome. Young infected berries turn gray-brown in a condition called grey rot or leather rot, then harden and shrivel. Late-season berry infections can show white sporulation under humid conditions.


## Favorable Conditions

Downy Mildew develops in temperatures between twelve and thirty degrees Celsius with an optimum between twenty and twenty-five degrees. Rain is required for oospore germination, for sporangia dispersal, and for zoospore release and movement to stomata. Overnight dew or rain provides the leaf wetness that enables infection. The disease is most damaging from flowering through veraison, the point of color change in berries. European wine grape varieties including Chardonnay, Merlot, Cabernet Sauvignon, and Riesling have essentially no meaningful resistance and are fully susceptible.


## Disease Cycle

Oospores overwinter in soil and infected leaf litter. Spring rains trigger oospore germination, producing macrosporangia that release zoospores capable of infecting leaves through stomata. Primary infection begins when the ten-ten-ten rule conditions are met. After an incubation of five to eighteen days, oil spots appear and white sporangial growth develops on the leaf underside. Rain and wind disperse new sporangia across the vineyard. Multiple infection cycles occur throughout the season during warm, wet weather. At season end, sexual reproduction between compatible strains produces oospores within infected tissue, which fall with leaves to the soil and survive until the following spring.


## Treatment

Canopy management is the most important cultural practice. Leaf removal and shoot positioning improve air circulation and reduce humidity around clusters, significantly lowering infection risk. Removing leaves around clusters at bloom directly reduces the risk of the most economically damaging cluster infection. Overhead irrigation should be avoided. Leaf litter should be shredded and incorporated at season end to reduce oospore survival. Rows should be oriented to maximize sunlight and airflow.

Variety choice is the most powerful long-term tool. American species and PIWI varieties, which are European-American hybrids bred for disease resistance, carry significant natural resistance. PIWI varieties such as Regent, Johanniter, Bronner, Solaris, Cabernet Blanc, and Soreli can reduce fungicide requirements by fifty to eighty percent compared to susceptible European varieties. Pure Vitis vinifera varieties have no meaningful resistance and require full chemical protection programs.

## Management

Chemical control must use only products specifically labeled for downy mildew and active against oomycetes, because products designed for true fungal diseases will not work. Protectant fungicides including mancozeb, folpet, and copper coat the leaf surface and prevent infection but provide no curative activity. They must be reapplied after significant rain. Systemic fungicides provide penetration into leaf tissue and some curative activity. Phenylamide products such as metalaxyl and mefenoxam have systemic action but resistance is widespread and they must never be used alone, always in premix with a protectant. Carboxylic acid amide products such as dimethomorph, iprovalicarb, and mandipropamid are systemic with excellent anti-sporulation activity and some curative action up to forty-eight hours after infection. Cymoxanil provides curative activity up to twenty-four hours post-infection. The spray program should begin when shoots reach ten centimeters or when the ten-ten-ten trigger is met, whichever comes first. Intervals are seven to ten days in wet weather and fourteen days in dry periods. Systemic fungicides should be prioritized at bloom for maximum protection of flowers. Metalaxyl and mefenoxam should be rotated with CAA products and cymoxanil-containing products, and should always be used only in premix formulations to manage resistance risk.

In organic viticulture, copper-based fungicides are the primary tool and can be effective protectants when applied on schedule. Potassium phosphite triggers plant defense responses and provides limited but real suppression and is approved in some organic programs. Bacillus subtilis has limited suppressive activity and plays a supportive role. Canopy management and PIWI variety selection are the most impactful organic strategies.


## Quick Actions

When shoots reach ten centimeters in a wet spring, begin the protectant fungicide program immediately without waiting for symptoms. When the ten-ten-ten rule is triggered, apply a systemic plus protectant tank mix before the next rain event. When oil spots become visible on leaves, apply a systemic fungicide immediately and increase spray frequency to every five to seven days. At the beginning of bloom, apply a systemic fungicide regardless of weather because this is the single most critical spray timing to protect flowers. When white sporulation is visible on lesions, indicating an active epidemic, spray every five to seven days and remove infected shoots.


## Scouting and Monitoring

Monitor shoots daily once they reach five centimeters because the ten-ten-ten trigger can arrive suddenly. Check both leaf surfaces at every scouting visit, looking for oil spots on the upper surface and white sporulation on the lower surface. Pay special attention to the youngest shoots and cluster zones. Track sporulation intensity because sporulating lesions indicate active epidemic spread and require an immediate response. After any rain event meeting the ten-ten-ten conditions, scout for oil spots five to ten days later. Regional disease forecasting systems can help optimize spray timing where available.


## Geographic Distribution

Downy Mildew originated in North America and was introduced to Europe in 1878 with near-catastrophic consequences for the wine industry. Today it is present in all grape-growing regions worldwide. It is most severe in humid continental and maritime climates such as France, Germany, Italy, the eastern United States, and China. In dry Mediterranean climates it causes less pressure but can still produce severe losses in wet years. It is also damaging in South America, South Africa, and Australia.
