# Bacterial Spot - Tomato

**Pathogen:** Xanthomonas perforans  
**Type:** Bacterial  
**Host:** Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Bacterial Spot of tomato is a bacterial disease caused by Xanthomonas perforans and related Xanthomonas species that attack tomato plants in warm, humid conditions. It is one of the most economically significant bacterial diseases of tomato worldwide, affecting leaves, stems, and fruit. The disease causes defoliation, which reduces fruit quality by exposing fruit to sunscald, and causes direct fruit lesions that significantly reduce marketability. It is a primary concern in commercial fresh-market tomato production in humid regions and is present wherever tomatoes are grown commercially in warm climates.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Bacterial Spot of tomato is rated as a high danger disease. In highly susceptible varieties during warm, wet seasons, complete loss of marketable fruit is possible. Defoliation is the primary driver of economic loss, as exposed fruit suffers sunscald and the plant loses photosynthetic capacity needed for fruit fill. Fruit lesions directly reduce grade and value for fresh market production. The disease can spread extremely rapidly during periods of warm, rainy weather, moving from first detected infections to widespread damage within one to two weeks.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
The incubation period is three to five days under warm, humid conditions. The very short incubation period allows for rapid epidemic development when weather is favorable. The disease is most active during warm summer periods with frequent rainfall or irrigation events. Infections can occur continuously throughout the growing season under favorable conditions, making management a continuous commitment once disease is established in the field.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
Xanthomonas perforans spreads primarily through wind-driven rain, overhead irrigation, infected transplants, and contaminated seed. The bacterium can survive for limited periods in soil and plant debris. Infected transplants are a common source of initial field introduction and represent the most critical point of prevention. Once established, the bacterium spreads through water splash from infected to healthy tissue during rain events and irrigation. Mechanical spread through field workers, equipment, and insects moving through wet foliage also contributes to within-field spread.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Xanthomonas perforans is a gram-negative rod-shaped bacterium and the predominant species causing Bacterial Spot of tomato in the southeastern United States and many other regions. Multiple races exist that differ in virulence on varieties with specific resistance genes. The pathogen produces effector proteins that suppress plant immune responses and facilitate colonization. Race populations have shifted in response to variety resistance gene deployment, requiring continuous monitoring of race composition in commercial production areas.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On leaves, Bacterial Spot causes small, water-soaked circular spots that enlarge to become irregular, brown lesions with yellow halos. Lesion centers may fall out as tissue dies, creating a ragged, shot-hole appearance. Severely infected leaves turn yellow and drop, exposing fruit and reducing photosynthetic capacity. On fruit, small, slightly raised, water-soaked spots develop that become brown, rough, and scab-like with age. Lesions are typically superficial but make fruit unmarketable for fresh sale. Secondary fungal pathogens often colonize bacterial lesions, causing additional damage. On stems and petioles, water-soaked elongated lesions may develop that cause tissue collapse and dieback above the infected area.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Bacterial Spot of tomato thrives in temperatures between twenty-four and thirty degrees Celsius and requires free water on leaf surfaces for spread and infection. Wind-driven rain and overhead irrigation are the most effective means of disease spread. High humidity that maintains leaf wetness for extended periods promotes rapid epidemic development. Transplants established under warm, humid conditions are highly susceptible to infection during the establishment period.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
The cycle begins with infected seed or transplants introduced into the field, or with inoculum surviving in crop debris from a previous season. Bacteria spread from initial infections during rain events and irrigation throughout the growing season. Each infection cycle takes three to five days under warm conditions, allowing for rapid epidemic buildup. Crop debris infected at the end of the season may provide limited inoculum for the following season, though survival outside a living host is limited.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
Cultural management includes using certified pathogen-free seed treated with hot water and certified disease-free transplants, implementing crop rotation with non-solanaceous crops for at least two years, switching to drip irrigation, and destroying crop debris at the end of the season. Inspection of transplants before field establishment is essential for preventing introduction. Field hygiene including washing hands and sanitizing equipment between fields reduces mechanical spread. Variety selection can reduce losses in regions where specific races predominate, but race shifts in pathogen populations have eroded the usefulness of single resistance genes. Varieties with multiple resistance genes or quantitative resistance provide more durable protection.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Chemical control relies on copper-based bactericides applied on a five to seven day schedule during warm, wet weather. Copper applications must begin at transplanting and be maintained throughout the season during favorable weather. Copper resistance is widespread in Xanthomonas populations in many production regions, reducing the efficacy of copper alone. Adding mancozeb to copper programs improves efficacy by reducing the rate of copper resistance development and providing an additional protective mode of action. Biological bactericides based on Bacillus subtilis or Pseudomonas fluorescens are available for use in conventional and organic programs as supplementary tools.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
Before transplanting, inspect transplants for lesions and reject symptomatic plants. At transplanting, apply protective copper bactericide. During warm, wet periods, maintain five to seven day spray intervals. When first lesions appear on leaves, apply copper immediately, reduce overhead irrigation, and avoid working in wet foliage. When fruit lesions are detected, assess economic impact and adjust harvest schedule to recover value from affected fruit before further deterioration.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Scout fields twice weekly from transplanting through harvest during warm, wet seasons. Examine the lower canopy and undersides of leaves for early lesions. Inspect transplants at establishment for initial infections. After rain events or irrigation, inspect within three to five days for new infections given the short incubation period. Track weather conditions and apply bactericides before anticipated rain events when disease is present.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Bacterial Spot of tomato is most severe in the warm, humid tomato-producing regions of the southeastern United States, parts of Latin America, and humid regions of Europe and Asia. It is present wherever tomatoes are grown commercially in warm climates and is particularly problematic in Florida, where it is a primary disease concern throughout the growing season.
