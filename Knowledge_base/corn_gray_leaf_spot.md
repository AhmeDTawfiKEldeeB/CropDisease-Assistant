# Gray Leaf Spot - Corn
Pathogen: Cercospora zeae-maydis | Type: Fungal | Host: Corn / Maize (Zea mays)


## Definition

Gray Leaf Spot is one of the most yield-limiting fungal diseases of corn worldwide, caused by Cercospora zeae-maydis. It produces long, rectangular, gray to tan lesions that run parallel to leaf veins, eventually causing entire leaves to blight and die. The disease is particularly destructive in no-till production systems where infected corn residue remains on the soil surface as the primary source of inoculum season after season. It thrives in warm, humid environments with prolonged overnight dew and poor air circulation through the canopy. Severe infections can destroy fifty percent or more of yield by killing the leaf canopy before grain fill is complete, and they also predispose plants to serious stalk rot at harvest.


## Severity and Danger Level

Gray Leaf Spot is rated as a high-danger disease. It does not kill plants directly, but severe defoliation before grain fill is complete prevents ears from developing normally and causes premature plant death through stalk rot. Yield losses range from twenty to fifty percent in severe cases, and losses exceeding fifty percent have been recorded in highly susceptible hybrids under ideal disease conditions. The disease reduces grain weight, test weight, and stalk integrity at harvest. Once lesions form, fungicides cannot reverse existing damage but can protect healthy remaining tissue.


## Time to Spread

Infection to visible symptoms takes fourteen to twenty-one days under optimal warm, humid conditions. The fungus spreads continuously through wind during the growing season, and spores produced within lesions generate new infection cycles. From the first lesions to severe canopy blighting takes three to six weeks under favorable conditions. The disease progresses at a moderate to fast pace and is cumulative, meaning that without intervention it tends to worsen steadily rather than plateau.


## How It Spreads

The primary source of Gray Leaf Spot inoculum is infected corn residue left on the soil surface. This is the defining characteristic that separates it from diseases whose inoculum arrives on wind from distant regions. The fungus survives as dormant structures in residue for one to two seasons and produces conidia during warm, humid conditions that wind carries to lower leaves. No-till and reduced-till fields have dramatically higher risk because residue is retained on the surface rather than buried. Once lesions establish on lower leaves, they produce new conidia that spread upward through the canopy progressively over the season. Long-distance spread by wind does occur across fields and regions during warm, humid weather, but local residue is always the dominant source.


## Pathogen Biology

Cercospora zeae-maydis is an ascomycete fungus that survives as stromata in corn residue on the soil surface. A closely related species, Cercospora zeina, has been identified particularly in African corn production. The fungus produces long, multiseptate conidia that are dispersed by wind. Infection requires penetration through stomata and depends on extended leaf wetness of at least twelve hours. The fungus produces a photosensitizing toxin called cercosporin that assists tissue colonization by generating reactive oxygen species in the presence of light. Residue on the soil surface can remain infectious for one to two growing seasons, which is why rotation away from corn reduces disease pressure over time.


## Symptoms

On leaves, Gray Leaf Spot begins as small tan lesions with yellow halos that rapidly expand into rectangular, gray to tan streaks one to six centimeters long. The lesion margins are defined by leaf veins, giving the characteristic rectangular shape that is the primary diagnostic feature of this disease. As the fungus sporulates, lesions develop a velvety gray appearance on the surface. Severe infection causes complete leaf blighting with premature plant death. Sheaths may also develop similar rectangular lesions in severe cases. The disease progresses upward through the canopy from lower to upper leaves, and the most critical timing is when it reaches the ear leaf and above before grain fill begins.


## Favorable Conditions

Gray Leaf Spot thrives in temperatures between twenty-two and thirty degrees Celsius with an optimum around twenty-five degrees. It requires at least twelve hours of continuous leaf wetness for infection, which is most commonly provided by prolonged overnight dew in dense canopies. High relative humidity throughout the night is a strong predictor of active infection. The disease is most severe during the period from tasseling through grain fill. No-till fields, dense plant populations, and late-planted corn are consistently the highest-risk situations. Fields in low-lying areas or river bottoms with poor air drainage are particularly vulnerable.


## Disease Cycle

The cycle begins with the fungus surviving the winter in corn residue on the soil surface. When warm, humid spring conditions arrive, conidia are produced on the residue and wind carries them to the lower leaves of young corn plants. Infection occurs during extended leaf wetness periods. Lesions develop and begin producing new conidia within fourteen to twenty-one days. The disease progresses upward through the canopy through repeated infection cycles driven by wind-dispersed spores and overnight dew. At the end of the season, the fungus returns to residue on the soil surface where it persists until the following season.


## Treatment

The most effective management tool is planting hybrids with high gray leaf spot resistance ratings. Hybrids rated four to seven on a nine-point scale provide meaningful protection, though no completely immune hybrid exists because resistance is polygenic and partial. In no-till fields with a history of severe disease, selecting resistant hybrids is especially important and should be the first management decision.

Cultural practices that reduce residue inoculum are highly valuable. Rotating to a non-host crop such as soybeans or small grains for one to two seasons allows residue to break down and substantially reduces the inoculum load. Tillage that buries residue reduces primary inoculum but must be weighed against erosion concerns. Avoiding excessively dense plant populations reduces canopy humidity. Adequate potassium fertility supports plant tolerance. Planting early allows the canopy to senesce before the worst humidity periods of late summer.

## Management

Fungicide applications are economically justified when disease reaches the ear leaf by tasseling in susceptible hybrids. The optimal timing is at VT to R1, meaning at tasseling through silking. Strobilurins such as azoxystrobin and pyraclostrobin provide good protectant activity. Triazoles such as propiconazole and tebuconazole offer systemic and some curative activity. SDHI products such as fluxapyroxad are excellent and are most effective when used in premix combinations with triazoles or strobilurins. One application at the correct timing is usually sufficient. A second application at R2 may be justified in severe epidemic years on highly susceptible hybrids. Applying after the R4 dough stage has negligible economic return. Fungicide modes of action should be rotated to reduce resistance development.

Biological options including Trichoderma-based products and silicon amendments have limited and inconsistent efficacy and are not primary management tools. Crop rotation and resistant varieties remain the most practical and cost-effective approach, especially in no-till production.


## Quick Actions

Before planting in a high-risk field such as no-till with previous corn, select a resistant hybrid and consider rotating to a non-host crop. From V6 to V10 when lesions appear on lower leaves, begin scouting every five days. When approaching tasseling with lesions on the ear leaf or above, apply fungicide immediately because this is the critical window. After tasseling with severe canopy blighting, apply fungicide if the crop is still between R2 and R3. After harvest in a severely affected field, consider deep tillage or rotation to reduce residue inoculum for the next season.


## Scouting and Monitoring

Begin scouting at V10 and intensify from V14 through tasseling. Sample a minimum of twenty plants per field at five randomly selected locations. Record the percentage of leaf area affected and note the highest leaf position showing lesions. Low-lying and foggy areas of fields often show disease earliest. Scout these areas first. The key threshold for fungicide application is gray leaf spot visible on the ear leaf approaching or at tasseling in a susceptible hybrid.


## Geographic Distribution

Gray Leaf Spot is a major disease in the Americas, particularly in the US Corn Belt and in Brazil and Argentina. It is also a significant problem in sub-Saharan Africa, especially in highland tropical maize production, and in parts of Asia including India and China. The disease is most destructive in warm, humid highland environments and in no-till production systems wherever corn is grown.
