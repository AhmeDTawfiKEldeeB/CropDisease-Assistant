# Spider Mites - Tomato
Pest: Tetranychus urticae (Two-Spotted Spider Mite) | Type: Arachnid | Host: Tomato and hundreds of other crops


## Definition

Spider mites are microscopic arachnids, not insects, belonging to the family Tetranychidae. The most economically important species on tomato is Tetranychus urticae, the two-spotted spider mite, named for two dark spots visible on the yellowish-green body of adults. At approximately half a millimeter in body length, they are barely visible to the naked eye and require a ten-times hand lens for reliable identification. They feed by piercing individual leaf cells and extracting the cellular contents, creating a characteristic dusty, stippled appearance on leaves that is often the first sign of infestation. In hot, dry weather they reproduce with extreme speed, with one female capable of producing over one hundred eggs and a population capable of doubling every three to five days. Their arachnid nature is critical to management because standard insecticides have little or no effect on them and often make infestations dramatically worse by killing the predatory insects and mites that naturally keep spider mite populations in check.


## Severity and Danger Level

Spider mites are rated as high danger in hot, dry conditions. Severe infestations can kill tomato plants within two to three weeks during summer heat. They cause significant yield loss through defoliation and direct feeding damage on fruit. The speed of population increase is extreme in hot, dry weather, making early detection and rapid response essential. Resistance to multiple miticide classes is widespread in commercial production areas, complicating chemical control.


## Time to Spread

The life cycle from egg to adult takes seven to fourteen days at twenty-seven degrees Celsius and as little as five days at thirty-eight degrees. A single female produces over one hundred eggs in her lifetime. The population doubles every three to five days under hot, dry conditions, meaning that a small, early infestation can become a catastrophic outbreak within two weeks if not addressed. Within a field, mites spread by ballooning on silk threads carried by wind and can move through an entire field in one to two weeks. Cool, humid weather drastically slows reproduction and allows natural enemies to recover.


## How It Spreads

Spider mites overwinter as adult females, which turn reddish-orange in color, in soil, plant debris, and weeds around the field. They emerge when temperatures exceed twelve to fifteen degrees Celsius in spring and begin feeding and reproducing on available plant material. Wind carries tiny mites on silk threads from plant to plant and from field to field, which is the main mechanism of long-distance spread. Workers and equipment carry mites on clothing and tools within and between operations. Infected transplants can introduce mites directly into a clean field at planting. Weeds in and around field margins serve as persistent reservoirs for spider mite populations throughout the season. The most common cause of severe outbreaks in commercial production is the use of broad-spectrum insecticides such as pyrethroids, which kill the predatory insects and mites that naturally regulate spider mite populations while having little effect on the mites themselves.


## Pest Biology

Tetranychus urticae is a member of the class Arachnida, possessing eight legs as an adult, compared to the six legs of insects. This biological distinction explains why insecticides, which are designed to affect insect-specific biological processes, have limited efficacy against spider mites. The two dark spots on adults are accumulated gut contents rather than pigmentation. Adults live approximately three to four weeks and females lay one hundred to two hundred eggs during their lifetime. Eggs are laid on the underside of leaves and are spherical, pale, and shiny. Larvae hatch with six legs and transition through two nymphal stages before reaching the eight-legged adult form. The entire life cycle can be completed in five days at thirty-eight degrees Celsius, which explains why populations explode so dramatically during heat waves. Over twenty generations can occur in a single growing season. The species has an extremely broad host range, infecting over eleven hundred plant species across more than one hundred and forty plant families.


## Symptoms

Early infestations produce tiny yellow or white stippling dots on the upper leaf surface, each dot representing a single cell drained by mite feeding. This stippling may initially be confused with thrips damage or nutrient deficiency, and confirmation requires checking the leaf underside for the mites themselves.

As populations grow, the stippling coalesces and leaves develop a bronze, silver, or dusty overall appearance from the accumulated cell damage across large areas of the leaf surface.

Heavy infestations produce fine silk webbing on leaf undersides and between leaves. This webbing confirms a large, established colony and indicates that populations are already at damaging levels. Leaves under heavy webbing turn entirely yellow and then brown and die, and the plant defoliates progressively from the bottom up.

In the most severe cases during summer heat, plants can collapse and die within two to three weeks of the infestation becoming established.


## Favorable Conditions

Hot temperatures between twenty-seven and thirty-eight degrees Celsius dramatically accelerate reproduction. Low humidity below fifty percent suppresses natural enemies and allows mite populations to grow unchecked. Drought-stressed plants are significantly more susceptible than well-watered plants because plant defenses are compromised and stressed tissue provides more concentrated nutrients. The disease is primarily a summer problem in field production and a year-round problem in greenhouses. The use of broad-spectrum insecticides is itself a major favorable condition because it eliminates natural biological controls.


## Pest Life Cycle

Overwintering adult females emerge from soil and debris in spring when temperatures exceed twelve to fifteen degrees Celsius. They begin laying eggs on leaf undersides immediately. Eggs hatch within three to five days at warm temperatures. Larvae feed and develop through two nymphal stages to reach adulthood within seven to fourteen days at twenty-seven degrees Celsius. Adults mate and females begin laying new eggs within hours of reaching maturity. As populations become dense, mites produce silk and disperse by ballooning on air currents to new plants. In late season and autumn, mated females develop the reddish-orange winter coloration and move to overwintering sites in soil, bark crevices, and plant debris.


## Treatment

Maintaining consistent, adequate irrigation is one of the most important cultural practices for spider mite management because drought-stressed plants are far more susceptible and recover more slowly from feeding damage. Well-watered plants show greater tolerance and support faster recovery after mite populations are brought under control.

Preserving natural enemies is the single most important practice for long-term spider mite management. Avoiding broad-spectrum insecticides including pyrethroids, organophosphates, and carbamates preserves the populations of predatory mites and insects that naturally regulate spider mite numbers. Eliminating these natural controls is the primary cause of serious mite outbreaks in commercial production.

Controlling weeds around field margins removes the reservoir populations that supply mites to the crop throughout the season. Removing the most heavily infested plant material reduces the population load. Reflective silver mulches on the soil surface disorient and deter mite colonization of plants.

Biological control using predatory mites is the preferred approach in integrated pest management programs. Phytoseiulus persimilis is a highly effective specialist predator of spider mites that requires spider mite prey to survive. Neoseiulus californicus is more heat-tolerant and can persist at lower prey densities, making it better for preventive releases. Amblyseius andersoni is very heat-tolerant and performs well in summer conditions. Combining Phytoseiulus persimilis for fast knockdown with Neoseiulus californicus for persistence provides the best results. Releases should be made at the first sign of infestation and no miticides should be applied after release for a minimum of four to six weeks.

## Management

Chemical control requires miticides or acaricides specifically labeled for spider mites. Standard insecticides must not be used. Abamectin is highly effective and works as a neurotoxin but has limited ovicidal activity. Spiromesifen has excellent activity against eggs and larvae but no adult knockdown and must be applied when eggs and juveniles are present. Etoxazole and hexythiazox are mite growth inhibitors effective against eggs and larvae but not adults and must be used early in the infestation. Fenpyroximate provides fast adult knockdown but no ovicidal activity. Bifenazate has good adult knockdown. These chemistries must be rotated with every application using the IRAC resistance group numbers as a guide because resistance develops extremely rapidly in spider mite populations. Two sequential applications five to seven days apart are needed to catch eggs hatching after the first treatment. Thorough coverage of leaf undersides is mandatory because that is where mites live and feed and top-only spraying is ineffective. Horticultural oil and insecticidal soap are contact killers that suffocate mites and eggs on direct contact, require excellent coverage, and are suitable for organic programs. They have no residual activity.


## Quick Actions

When first stippling appears on a few leaves, apply predatory mites if available and apply neem oil or insecticidal soap as a bridge treatment. When bronze or silver leaves with webbing are present, apply a miticide immediately with thorough underside coverage and check the entire field for spread. When hot, dry weather above thirty degrees Celsius coincides with any stippling, act immediately because populations will double every three to five days in that weather. After any broad-spectrum insecticide application, expect a mite outbreak and monitor daily. When plants are heavily infested, remove and destroy the worst plants and treat surrounding plants with both miticide and predatory mite releases.


## Scouting and Monitoring

Scout weekly from transplanting and increase to twice weekly during hot, dry weather above twenty-seven degrees Celsius. Sample a minimum of thirty leaves per field from multiple locations throughout the field. Focus on older mid-canopy leaves and field margins where infestations typically start. Use a ten-times hand lens to count motile mites per leaf. An economic threshold of five to ten motile mites per leaf is commonly used for field crops as a trigger for treatment. In greenhouses, treat when mites are first detected on ten percent of plants. Check for the presence of predatory mites before deciding on chemical treatment. If predatory mites are active and populations are not increasing, it may be possible to hold off on chemical applications and allow biological control to take effect.


## Geographic Distribution

Spider mites are present globally on every continent where crops are grown. They are a major pest in Mediterranean Europe, the Middle East, North Africa, the United States, Latin America, and Southeast Asia. They are a year-round problem in tropical and subtropical regions and a seasonal summer problem in temperate climates. In greenhouses, populations are present year-round regardless of outdoor climate.
