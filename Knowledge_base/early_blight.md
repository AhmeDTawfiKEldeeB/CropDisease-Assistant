# Early Blight - Potato
Pathogen: Alternaria solani | Type: Fungal | Host: Potato (Solanum tuberosum), also Tomato


## Definition

Early Blight is a fungal disease of potato and tomato caused by Alternaria solani. It is one of the most common and widely distributed foliar diseases of potato worldwide. The disease creates dark spots with distinctive concentric rings that form a bullseye or target pattern, beginning on the oldest lower leaves and progressing steadily upward through the plant. Beyond leaf damage, Early Blight also infects potato tubers, producing sunken corky spots that reduce marketability and increase storage losses. The disease is significantly worsened by plant stress, especially from low nitrogen or drought, and is considered a mid to late season disease that builds as the canopy closes and plants begin to age.


## Severity and Danger Level

Early Blight is rated as moderate to high danger depending on crop condition and management. It rarely kills plants directly, but severe defoliation dramatically reduces tuber yield by cutting off photosynthesis before bulking is complete. Yield losses in severe, unmanaged cases range from twenty to forty percent. Tuber quality is also reduced when surface lesions occur, creating entry points for secondary pathogens during storage. The disease is significantly more damaging on stressed crops, particularly those suffering from nitrogen deficiency or drought, making plant nutrition and irrigation management inseparable from disease management.


## Time to Spread

The incubation period is two to three days in warm, humid conditions and extends to up to seven days in cooler weather. The disease builds from mid-season onward, worsening after canopy closure when humidity within the canopy increases. It progresses from the oldest leaves upward over three to six weeks under favorable conditions. The speed of progression is moderate under normal circumstances but accelerates dramatically when plants are under nitrogen or water stress.


## How It Spreads

Early Blight overwinters in infected plant debris in the soil and on infected seed tubers. Conidia are dispersed by wind and rain splash from lesions and from debris on the soil surface. The fungus enters host tissue through stomata or by directly penetrating weakened cuticle, especially on older and stressed tissue. Drought stress and low nitrogen dramatically increase susceptibility by weakening plant defenses. Each new lesion produces thousands of fresh conidia within days, providing continuous secondary inoculum throughout the season. Spores can also travel by wind between fields over significant distances.


## Pathogen Biology

Alternaria solani is an ascomycete fungus that survives in infected plant debris in soil and on infected seed tubers for one to two seasons. It produces large, club-shaped, multicellular conidia with a characteristic long beak, which are readily carried by wind. The fungus also produces toxins including altersolanol and solanopyrone that contribute to tissue necrosis by killing cells ahead of the advancing mycelium. This toxin production explains why lesions expand rapidly even when the fungus itself has not yet colonized the full lesion area. The pathogen infects both potato and tomato and can move between the two crops, making proximity of these crops a risk factor.


## Symptoms

On leaves, Early Blight produces dark brown to black lesions with distinct concentric rings that create the classic bullseye or target pattern. A yellow chlorotic halo surrounds each lesion. Lesions range from three to fifteen millimeters in diameter. The disease begins exclusively on older, lower leaves and progresses upward over the season. Severely infected leaves turn yellow and drop, reducing the photosynthetic capacity of the plant.

On stems, dark elongated sunken lesions can appear, occasionally girdling young stems and causing shoot death. Petiole lesions may cause premature leaf drop before the leaf itself is fully killed.

On tubers, Early Blight produces dark, sunken, circular to irregular corky lesions on the surface that are five to fifteen millimeters in diameter with slightly raised margins. Below the skin, a shallow, dry, corky rot is visible. These lesions reduce marketability significantly and serve as entry points for secondary pathogens during storage.


## Favorable Conditions

Early Blight thrives in temperatures between twenty-four and twenty-nine degrees Celsius with an optimum around twenty-seven degrees. High humidity and periods of leaf wetness are required for infection. The disease is most damaging on senescent, stressed, or nitrogen-deficient plants. It builds particularly after canopy closure when humidity within the crop increases. Dense plantings and mid to late season timing are consistent risk factors.


## Disease Cycle

The cycle begins with the fungus overwintering in infected plant debris in the soil and on infected seed tubers. When warm, humid conditions arrive in the growing season, conidia are released from debris and dispersed by wind and rain splash. Infection occurs through stomata or directly through weakened cuticle on older tissue. After an incubation of two to seven days, new lesions form and immediately begin producing fresh conidia. Multiple cycles repeat throughout the season. At the end of the season, infected debris falls to the soil and perpetuates the cycle, while infected tubers can also carry the pathogen into the next season and into storage.


## Treatment

Plant nutrition management is the most critical non-chemical tool for Early Blight. Maintaining adequate nitrogen throughout the season is essential because nitrogen-deficient plants develop far more severe disease under the same inoculum levels as well-fertilized plants. Potassium adequacy also supports disease tolerance. Consistent irrigation to prevent drought stress is equally important, and drip irrigation is preferred over overhead systems to keep foliage dry.

Crop rotation away from potato, tomato, and other solanaceous hosts for two to three seasons reduces debris inoculum in the soil. Certified disease-free seed tubers should always be used. Removing infected plant debris after harvest and early haulm destruction before harvest reduce tuber infection pressure.

## Management

Fungicide applications should begin when disease first appears or when conditions consistently favor infection, which means warm temperatures combined with humid nights. Applications every seven to ten days are typical, increasing to every five to seven days during wet, warm periods. Multi-site protectants such as chlorothalonil and mancozeb are applied preventively. Systemic fungicides including strobilurins such as azoxystrobin and pyraclostrobin, triazoles such as difenoconazole and tebuconazole, and SDHI products provide both preventive and curative activity and are valuable when disease is already present. Modes of action must be rotated with every two applications because strobilurin resistance in Alternaria populations is increasingly documented. Thorough coverage of lower canopy leaves is important because this is where the disease initiates.

In organic programs, copper-based fungicides are the most reliable option. Bacillus subtilis products have some suppressive activity and can support an integrated program. Stress management through nutrition and irrigation remains the most impactful organic intervention.


## Quick Actions

When bullseye spots appear on lower leaves, apply fungicide and check nitrogen levels immediately. When plants show drought stress alongside disease, irrigate first and then apply fungicide after irrigation. Before hot weather above twenty-four degrees combined with humid nights, apply a protectant fungicide in advance. When disease is spreading rapidly up the canopy, switch to a systemic fungicide and address all stress factors simultaneously. At harvest, when tubers show corky spots, assess and improve the rotation plan and storage conditions for the following season.


## Scouting and Monitoring

Begin scouting at canopy closure and intensify mid-season. Sample twenty to thirty plants per field from multiple locations. Record the percentage of plants with symptoms, the highest leaf position affected, and the number of lesions per leaf. Check the oldest leaves at the base of the plant first because disease always begins there. If leaf color is pale or yellow alongside Early Blight symptoms, nitrogen correction is urgent. Track weather periods of three or more consecutive days with temperatures above twenty-four degrees combined with rain or heavy dew, as these represent high-risk windows.


## Geographic Distribution

Early Blight is present globally wherever potato and tomato are grown. It is most severe in warm, humid continental and subtropical climates. In temperate regions it is a mid to late season disease. In tropical production regions it operates as a year-round problem. It is consistently among the most common foliar diseases reported by potato growers worldwide.
