# Esca (Black Measles) - Grape
Pathogens: Phaeomoniella chlamydospora, Phaeoacremonium minimum, Fomitiporia mediterranea | Type: Fungal Wood Disease Complex | Host: Grapevine (Vitis spp.)


## Definition

Esca is a complex and incurable wood disease of grapevines caused by a consortium of fungi that slowly colonize and rot the internal woody tissue of the trunk and arms. It has been recognized in vineyards for over two thousand years and remains one of the most serious threats to mature vines worldwide. On the outside, the disease produces a striking tiger-stripe pattern on leaves and dark spots on berries known as black measles. Internally, the wood decays progressively until the vine's vascular system can no longer function. In its most severe acute form, called apoplexy, a vine can collapse and die within two to five days during a summer heat wave. Because the wood cannot be disinfected once infected, Esca is defined by prevention rather than cure.


## Severity and Danger Level

Esca is rated as very high danger and is unique in being both chronic and incurable. It can kill grapevines through two distinct pathways. The chronic form causes gradual decline over years as internal wood decay reduces the vine's ability to transport water and nutrients. The acute form, apoplexy, causes sudden total collapse of the vine within days when heat stress pushes a severely infected vine past its physiological limit. Yield loss is significant every year in symptomatic vines, and apoplexy cases result in one hundred percent loss for the affected vine. The global economic cost of Esca is estimated in the billions of dollars annually. Mature vineyards older than fifteen years typically show incidence rates of five to thirty percent, which can reach fifty percent or more in severely affected blocks.


## Time to Spread

Within an individual vine, the fungi spread slowly through woody tissue over months to years. External symptoms may not appear for three to ten years after initial infection, meaning a vine can be diseased long before any visible sign is present. Between vines, spores spread at pruning time through wind and contaminated tools. The chronic disease form is very slow, developing over many seasons. The apoplectic form, in contrast, is extremely sudden once triggered by heat stress, with complete vine collapse occurring within two to five days.


## How It Spreads

Esca spreads primarily through fresh pruning wounds. When cuts are made in the vine, the exposed wood surface is colonized by airborne spores within hours. These spores are produced on infected wood in the vineyard and travel on wind to land on freshly cut surfaces. Contaminated pruning tools are an equally important vector. When an infected vine is cut and the same unsterilized tool is used on a healthy vine, the fungi are transferred directly in the sap on the blade. Infected nursery stock is a major source of new vineyard infections, as vines can carry the pathogen internally without showing any symptoms at planting. Esca does not spread through soil, through root contact, or through irrigation water. Once inside the vine, the fungi grow progressively through the woody tissue toward the roots over many seasons.


## Pathogen Biology

Esca is caused by three main fungal species acting together as a complex. Phaeomoniella chlamydospora is an early colonizer that causes brown and black staining of the vascular tissue. Phaeoacremonium minimum, formerly known as P. aleophilum, is a vascular pathogen that causes the characteristic esca proper. Fomitiporia mediterranea is a basidiomycete that causes white spongy rot in the older central wood. These fungi produce spores under wet conditions that are released and carried by wind to pruning wounds. They survive for extended periods inside woody tissue and in infected pruning stubs. Their slow growth through wood explains the long latency period between infection and symptom expression.


## Symptoms

On leaves, Esca produces interveinal chlorosis with reddish-brown necrotic spots that create the distinctive tiger stripe pattern. Leaf margins may dry, brown, and curl inward. These symptoms typically appear suddenly in mid-summer and may vary year to year, with symptomatic vines sometimes appearing healthy the following season.

On berries, small dark purple to black spots appear on the berry surface with lighter halos. These spots are the black measles for which the disease is named. Affected berries crack and shrivel at the spots, making entire clusters unmarketable.

In the wood, cutting across the trunk reveals the most reliable diagnostic signs. Brown or black staining in the vascular tissue and white, soft, spongy rot in the older central wood confirm Esca. The infected wood has a characteristic dirty or decayed texture and may show gummosis at pruning wounds.

Apoplexy presents as sudden wilting and death of all shoots on one or more arms, or the entire vine, within two to five days. The foliage collapses as if from severe drought but does not recover when irrigated. Cutting the trunk confirms infection through the dark internal staining.


## Favorable Conditions

Esca is primarily a disease of mature vines. Vines under five years old are rarely affected. Older vines become progressively more susceptible as their woody structure accumulates pruning wounds and internal decay. Infection occurs at pruning time, particularly when wounds are exposed to rain or wet conditions shortly after cutting. Heat waves in summer trigger apoplexy in vines that already carry substantial internal fungal load. Drought, heat, and nutrient deficiency worsen the probability and severity of apoplexy. The disease is present worldwide but is worst in warm, Mediterranean, and semi-arid climates.


## Disease Cycle

The cycle begins when airborne spores from infected wood or nursery stock land on fresh pruning wounds. The fungi colonize the wound surface and grow into the woody tissue progressively over months and years. The internal wood decays slowly while the vine continues to function. Eventually the fungal load disrupts vascular function enough to produce external symptoms. In summers with extreme heat, the infected vascular system fails catastrophically, causing apoplexy. Infected pruning stubs in the vineyard continuously produce spores during wet weather throughout the year, infecting adjacent vines season after season.


## Treatment and Management

Because there is no cure once the wood is infected, all management is preventive. The most important practice is protecting pruning wounds immediately after every cut. Wound protectants including thiophanate-methyl fungicide paint and formulations containing Trichoderma harzianum should be applied to every cut surface within hours of pruning. These products colonize the wound and exclude the Esca pathogens before they can establish. Pruning tools must be sterilized between every vine using ten percent bleach solution or seventy percent alcohol. Carrying two pairs of shears and alternating between them while the other soaks in sterilant is a practical method. Pruning during dry weather reduces spore release. Pruning during the coldest part of winter, when fungal activity is lowest, reduces infection risk. Double pruning, which involves a rough cut in autumn followed by the final cut in late winter, minimizes the time wounds are exposed during high-risk periods.

New vineyards should be established only with certified disease-free planting material. Every vine should be inspected at the graft union and a cross-section of the wood should be examined for dark staining before accepting any nursery material.

When infection is discovered early, surgical interventions can extend vine life. Trunk renewal removes the infected trunk entirely and trains a new shoot from the rootstock base. This is effective when disease has not yet reached the roots and typically requires three to five years to restore full production. Curettage involves surgically carving out infected wood from the trunk and sealing the wound with a biological protectant. Removing individual infected arms while the trunk remains is feasible for early or localized infections.

No systemic fungicide eliminates established infections inside the wood. Chemical treatments are effective only as wound protectants applied immediately at the time of pruning.


## Quick Actions

When about to prune, prepare sterilization solution before starting and apply wound protectant to every cut immediately after making it. When tiger-stripe leaf symptoms appear mid-summer, mark affected vines with flagging tape, assess the number of affected vines, and plan trunk renewal for vines that have shown symptoms for two or more consecutive years. When a vine collapses suddenly, cut the trunk to confirm internal staining, remove the vine, and evaluate the surrounding vines for early symptoms. When planting a new vineyard, inspect every vine's wood cross-section before accepting the material and refuse any showing dark staining.


## Scouting and Monitoring

Survey the entire vineyard annually in mid-summer, which is July or August in the northern hemisphere, when foliar symptoms are most visible. Record all symptomatic vines by location using GPS or a vineyard map. Track the progression of each marked vine year over year to identify those worsening consistently. During dormant pruning, cross-section flagged vines to confirm internal staining and assess how deep the decay has progressed. Track apoplexy events during summer heat waves as a measure of the disease's severity in the block.


## Geographic Distribution

Esca is present in all grape-growing regions worldwide. It is most economically damaging in France, Italy, Spain, Portugal, California, and Australia. Incidence in mature vineyards typically ranges from five to thirty percent and can exceed fifty percent in the most affected blocks. The disease became significantly more prevalent in European vineyards after sodium arsenite, a highly effective wood protectant, was banned in 2002.


## Frequently Asked Questions

Can I cure a vine that already has Esca? No. Once the wood is infected, no chemical or biological treatment eliminates the fungi from inside the tissue. Management focuses on slowing progression through surgical interventions and protecting healthy vines.

My vine suddenly wilted and died in three days. Is this Esca? This sounds like apoplexy, the acute form of Esca triggered by heat stress in an already-infected vine. Cut the trunk. If you see brown or black wood staining, Esca is confirmed.

How do I know if my pruning tools are spreading the disease? You cannot tell visually. Assume contamination and sterilize between every vine, especially in vineyards with known Esca history.

My vineyard is new. Can I get Esca? Yes, through infected nursery stock. This is why certified disease-free planting material is essential. Always inspect wood cross-sections when accepting new vines.

Does Esca spread from vine to vine through the soil? Not typically. The main spread is through pruning wounds via airborne spores and contaminated tools, not through soil or root contact.
