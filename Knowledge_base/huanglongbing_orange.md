# Huanglongbing / Citrus Greening - Orange

**Pathogen:** Candidatus Liberibacter asiaticus  
**Type:** Bacterial  
**Host:** Orange (Citrus sinensis)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Huanglongbing, also known as Citrus Greening or HLB, is a bacterial disease caused by Candidatus Liberibacter asiaticus that is widely regarded as the most destructive citrus disease in the world. It is spread by the Asian citrus psyllid insect and has no known cure. Once a tree is infected, it is infected for life and will eventually decline and die. The disease disrupts the phloem tissue of the tree, preventing the normal transport of nutrients and carbohydrates. Infected trees produce small, lopsided, bitter, and commercially worthless fruit. The disease has devastated commercial citrus production in Florida, parts of Asia, and threatens all citrus-growing regions where the psyllid vector is present.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Huanglongbing is rated as the highest danger citrus disease known. It is incurable, systemic, and fatal to every commercial citrus variety. There is no known resistant citrus variety. A single infected tree can serve as a source from which the psyllid vector spreads the disease to surrounding trees. In Florida, HLB has reduced citrus production by over eighty percent since its detection in 2005. The disease kills infected trees within five to ten years of infection under commercial production conditions, though the timeline can be shortened by additional stress factors. The economic impact is catastrophic wherever the disease establishes.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
The incubation period after infection by the psyllid vector ranges from several months to over one year before symptoms become visible. This long latent period is one of the most dangerous aspects of HLB, as infected trees appear healthy while serving as inoculum sources for psyllid-mediated spread. Once symptoms appear, tree decline accelerates over one to several years. The disease spreads through a grove at a rate determined by psyllid population density and the proportion of infected trees serving as inoculum sources.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
Huanglongbing is spread almost exclusively by the Asian citrus psyllid, Diaphorina citri. Psyllids acquire the bacterium by feeding on phloem tissue of an infected tree and can then transmit it to healthy trees during subsequent feeding. The bacterium is not seed-transmitted under most conditions and does not spread through soil, water, or air contact between trees. Infected budwood used for grafting spreads the disease over long distances and was responsible for much of the initial global spread. Psyllid populations must be managed to slow the spread of the disease within and between groves.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Candidatus Liberibacter asiaticus is a gram-negative, phloem-limited bacterium that cannot be cultured outside a living host. It colonizes phloem sieve tubes throughout the tree, disrupting carbohydrate and nutrient transport. The bacterium is transmitted in a persistent, circulative manner by the Asian citrus psyllid, meaning that the psyllid acquires the bacterium, it multiplies within the psyllid, and the psyllid remains infectious for the rest of its life. The bacterium is also present in the salivary glands of infected psyllids and is inoculated into phloem tissue during feeding.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On leaves, Huanglongbing causes a distinctive asymmetric or blotchy mottling pattern called blotchy mottle that does not correspond to the vein pattern of the leaf. This pattern distinguishes HLB from nutritional deficiencies, which typically produce symmetric patterns. Leaves on affected branches may show yellow shoot, where an entire branch or flush of growth turns yellow. On fruit, infected trees produce small, lopsided fruit that remain partially green at the stylar end even when mature, giving the disease the name Citrus Greening. The fruit is bitter, poorly flavored, and commercially worthless. Seeds within infected fruit are often brown and aborted. On the whole tree, advanced HLB causes general decline with sparse canopy, dieback of branches, small undersized fruit, and eventual death of the tree over several years.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Huanglongbing can infect citrus under all climate conditions where the Asian citrus psyllid survives. Psyllid populations are highest during warm weather and during periods of new vegetative flush, which is the primary feeding and egg-laying site for the insect. High psyllid populations combined with a high proportion of infected source trees accelerate disease spread within a grove. The bacterium has no particular temperature or humidity requirements beyond those needed for the host tree and vector to survive.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
The cycle begins with psyllids feeding on infected citrus tissue and acquiring the bacterium. After a latent period within the psyllid, the insect becomes infectious and transmits the bacterium to healthy trees during phloem feeding. The bacterium multiplies within the new host and spreads systemically through the phloem over months to years. As more trees become infected, they serve as inoculum sources for psyllids that continue to spread the disease. There is no recovery or cure once infection is established.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
There is no cure for Huanglongbing. Management focuses on slowing the spread of the disease by reducing psyllid populations and removing infected trees that serve as inoculum sources. Aggressive psyllid control through insecticide applications is the primary tool available to commercial growers. Infected trees should be removed and destroyed as quickly as possible after confirmation to reduce inoculum pressure. Thermotherapy, trunk injection of antibiotics such as oxytetracycline, and nutritional management programs have shown some ability to temporarily reduce symptom severity and improve fruit production in infected trees, but none of these treatments eliminate the pathogen or restore tree health to pre-infection levels. Research into resistant rootstocks and varieties is ongoing but has not yet produced commercially viable solutions.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Integrated management programs combine intensive psyllid monitoring and control, rapid removal of symptomatic trees, use of certified pathogen-free nursery stock, and nutritional support programs for infected trees that growers choose to maintain in production. Psyllid control relies on systemic insecticides applied to soil or foliage, with soil drenches providing longer residual activity. The goal of psyllid control is to reduce vector populations below thresholds that allow rapid disease spread. Entire region-wide coordinated management programs have been established in Florida and other affected areas to address the landscape-scale nature of the disease.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
If yellow shoot symptoms or blotchy mottle is observed, submit samples for laboratory confirmation immediately. While awaiting results, apply a systemic insecticide to control psyllids on affected and adjacent trees. Upon laboratory confirmation of HLB, notify local agricultural authorities as required. Remove and destroy confirmed infected trees as quickly as possible to reduce inoculum. Increase psyllid monitoring frequency on remaining trees and apply insecticides on a regular schedule.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Scout trees monthly for blotchy mottle symptoms on leaves and yellowing shoots. During periods of vegetative flush, inspect new growth for psyllid eggs and nymphs. Submit suspect samples to a certified laboratory for polymerase chain reaction testing to confirm HLB, as visual diagnosis alone is insufficient. Track psyllid populations using sticky traps and direct inspection. Participate in area-wide monitoring programs where available.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Huanglongbing is present in virtually all citrus-producing regions of Asia, where it originated. It is established in Florida in the United States, Brazil, and parts of Africa. It has been detected in California and Texas and is considered a severe threat to all citrus-producing regions of the Americas and the Mediterranean where the Asian citrus psyllid is present or could become established.
