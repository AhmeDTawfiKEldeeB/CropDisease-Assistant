# Bacterial Spot - Pepper

**Pathogen:** Xanthomonas euvesicatoria  
**Type:** Bacterial  
**Host:** Pepper (Capsicum annuum)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Bacterial Spot of pepper is a bacterial disease caused by Xanthomonas euvesicatoria that attacks pepper plants in warm, humid conditions. It is one of the most destructive bacterial diseases of both pepper and tomato, causing lesions on leaves, stems, and fruit that reduce yield and marketability. The disease thrives in conditions of warm temperatures combined with high humidity and frequent rainfall, making it a persistent problem in humid pepper-growing regions worldwide. Severe infections cause extensive defoliation that exposes fruit to sunscald and reduces the photosynthetic capacity needed for fruit development.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Bacterial Spot of pepper is rated as a high danger disease. In susceptible varieties during warm, wet seasons, complete crop loss is possible. Defoliation exposes fruit to sunscald and reduces fruit size and quality. Fruit lesions directly reduce marketability for fresh market production. The disease can spread very rapidly through a field during periods of warm, rainy weather and can move from the first detected infections to widespread crop damage within one to two weeks under highly favorable conditions.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
The incubation period is three to five days under optimal conditions of warm temperatures and high humidity. The disease can spread through an entire field within a few weeks during favorable weather because of the very short time between infection and new inoculum production. The most critical period is from transplanting through the end of the growing season, with warm, wet midsummer weather producing the most rapid spread.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
Xanthomonas euvesicatoria is spread primarily through wind-driven rain, overhead irrigation, and infected transplants or seed. The bacterium can survive for limited periods in soil and on plant debris. Infected transplants introduced into the field are a common source of initial inoculation. Once established, the bacterium spreads rapidly during rain events and irrigation through water splash from lesions on infected tissue to healthy tissue. It enters plants through stomata, hydathodes, and wounds. Mechanical spread through field workers and equipment moving through wet foliage also occurs.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Xanthomonas euvesicatoria is a gram-negative rod-shaped bacterium closely related to the pathogen causing Bacterial Spot of tomato. Multiple races of the pathogen exist that differ in virulence on pepper varieties with specific resistance genes. The bacterium produces extracellular enzymes and effector proteins that suppress plant immunity and promote colonization of intercellular spaces in leaf and fruit tissue. It survives on infected seed and plant debris but does not persist for extended periods in soil without a host.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On leaves, Bacterial Spot causes small, water-soaked circular to irregular spots that become yellow-green with age and then turn brown with yellow halos. The center of lesions may fall out as tissue dies, creating a shot-hole appearance similar to that seen on peach. Severely infected leaves turn yellow and drop, exposing fruit to sunscald. On fruit, water-soaked spots develop that become raised, brown, and rough-textured with age. Fruit lesions may crack and become corky. Secondary fungal infections often establish in bacterial lesions, further reducing fruit quality. Severely infected fruit are unmarketable. On stems, elongated water-soaked lesions can develop that may crack and cause dieback of stem tissue above the infected area.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Bacterial Spot of pepper thrives in temperatures between twenty-four and thirty degrees Celsius. The bacterium requires free water on leaf surfaces for spread and infection, making overhead irrigation and rain events the primary drivers of epidemic development. High humidity that keeps leaf surfaces wet for extended periods increases infection success. Transplants grown under warm, humid greenhouse conditions are often infected before field establishment.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
The cycle begins with infected seed or transplants introduced into the field, or with inoculum surviving in crop debris from a previous season. Bacteria spread from initial infection sites during rain events and irrigation. Multiple infection cycles occur throughout the season, with each cycle taking three to five days under warm conditions. Late-season infections establish inoculum that may survive in soil and debris to infect the following season, though survival outside a living host is limited.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
Cultural management includes using certified disease-free seed and transplants, practicing crop rotation away from solanaceous crops for at least two years, avoiding overhead irrigation, and removing and destroying infected plant debris at the end of the season. Purchasing transplants from reputable nurseries that use hot water seed treatment and clean production practices is essential for preventing introduction. Field workers should avoid moving through wet foliage and should wash hands and sanitize tools between fields. Variety selection can reduce but not eliminate losses. Some pepper varieties carry resistance to specific races of the pathogen, but race variation means that resistance can be overcome when a new race is introduced.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Chemical control relies primarily on copper-based bactericides applied on a seven to ten day schedule during warm, wet weather. Applications should begin at transplanting or at the first sign of disease and be maintained throughout favorable weather periods. Resistance to copper has developed in many Xanthomonas populations and is widespread in some regions, making copper applications less reliable than in the past. Where copper resistance is confirmed, rotating or combining copper with mancozeb can provide some improvement. No bactericide eliminates established infections; chemical control is purely protective.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
Before transplanting, inspect transplants for lesions and reject any showing symptoms. At transplanting, apply a protective copper bactericide. During warm, wet weather, maintain seven-day spray intervals without exception. When first lesions appear on leaves, apply copper immediately and reduce overhead irrigation. When fruit lesions are detected, assess economic impact and adjust harvest timing if possible to recover value before further deterioration.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Scout fields twice weekly from transplanting through harvest during warm, wet seasons. Inspect the lower canopy and undersides of leaves where disease is typically first detected. Examine transplants at establishment for early lesions. After rain events or irrigation, increase inspection frequency to detect new infections within the three to five day incubation window. Track weather conditions and apply bactericides proactively before rain events when disease is present in the field.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Bacterial Spot of pepper is most severe in warm, humid pepper-growing regions including the southeastern United States, parts of Latin America, and humid regions of Europe and Asia. It is present wherever peppers are grown commercially in warm climates and is one of the primary constraints to pepper production in humid tropical and subtropical regions worldwide.
