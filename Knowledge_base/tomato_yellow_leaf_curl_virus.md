# Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus - Tomato

**Pathogen:** Tomato yellow leaf curl virus (TYLCV)  
**Type:** Viral  
**Host:** Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus is a viral disease caused by Tomato yellow leaf curl virus, a member of the Begomovirus genus transmitted by the silverleaf whitefly, Bemisia tabaci. It is one of the most destructive viral diseases of tomato in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide, capable of causing complete crop loss when infection occurs early in the season. The disease causes severe stunting, leaf curling and yellowing, and flower drop that results in negligible fruit production in heavily infected plants. Unlike mechanically transmitted viruses, TYLCV requires the whitefly vector to spread, making whitefly management the central element of disease control.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus is rated as a very high danger disease. Complete crop loss is possible when plants are infected during the first three to four weeks after transplanting. Early-infected plants produce little or no fruit regardless of subsequent management. Even when infection occurs later in the season, yield losses of thirty to eighty percent are common depending on the timing and percentage of plants affected. The combination of complete plant collapse in early-infected plants and high whitefly vector populations in warm climates makes this one of the most devastating viral threats to tomato production in affected regions.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
The incubation period after whitefly inoculation is one to three weeks before symptoms become visible. Whiteflies can acquire the virus after feeding on infected plants for as little as fifteen to thirty minutes and can transmit it to healthy plants after a latent period of eight to twenty-four hours within the insect. Once infected, a whitefly remains viruliferous for its entire life. The speed of field spread depends on whitefly population density and movement. Under high whitefly pressure, the majority of plants in a field can become infected within two to four weeks.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus is spread exclusively by the silverleaf whitefly, Bemisia tabaci, in a persistent, circulative manner. The virus does not spread through mechanical contact, seed, soil, or irrigation water. Whiteflies acquire the virus by feeding on infected plants and transmit it to healthy plants during subsequent phloem feeding. The virus circulates within the whitefly body and is inoculated into new host plants through saliva during feeding. Infected transplants introduced into the field before symptoms are visible are an important source of initial field introduction. Weeds and ornamental plants that serve as TYLCV reservoir hosts maintain whitefly-virus complexes between crop cycles.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Tomato yellow leaf curl virus is a single-stranded circular DNA virus with a geminate virion structure characteristic of the Begomovirus genus. It is transmitted in a persistent, circulative manner by Bemisia tabaci biotype B and Q, among others. The virus replicates in phloem-associated cells and moves systemically through the plant via the phloem. Multiple TYLCV strains and related Begomovirus species exist worldwide, and some strains have become recombinant, complicating resistance management. The virus can infect numerous host plant species beyond tomato, including many common weeds.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On infected plants, Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus causes upward curling and cupping of leaves beginning with young leaves at the growing point. Affected leaves show interveinal yellowing and a reduced, puckered texture. The entire plant may appear pale green to yellow and stunted. On early-infected plants, severe stunting results in a compact, bushy appearance. Internodes are short and leaves are small and severely distorted. Flower production is minimal and flowers that form drop before setting fruit. On later-infected plants, the lower canopy may appear relatively normal while the upper portion shows the characteristic curling and yellowing. Fruit set on lower trusses may be partially salvageable.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus is favored by conditions that support large whitefly populations: warm temperatures, dry conditions, and the availability of suitable host plants. Whitefly populations build rapidly during hot, dry weather and crash during cold winters. The disease is most severe in warm, dry production seasons when whitefly populations are highest and in regions where year-round warm weather prevents seasonal reduction in vector populations. Proximity to other whitefly host crops and weed reservoirs increases disease pressure.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
The cycle begins with viruliferous whiteflies migrating into tomato fields from infected weeds, ornamentals, or neighboring crops. These whiteflies inoculate tomato plants during feeding, and infected plants develop symptoms within one to three weeks. As plants become infected, they serve as new inoculum sources for whiteflies that feed on them and then move to healthy plants. The cycle continues throughout the growing season as long as whitefly populations remain active. In warm climates, the cycle is year-round, with no seasonal break.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
There is no curative treatment for TYLCV-infected plants. Infected plants should be removed and destroyed to reduce inoculum and prevent further whitefly acquisition and spread. Roguing is most valuable in the early season before widespread infection has occurred. Variety selection is the most effective long-term management strategy. Varieties with the Ty-1, Ty-2, Ty-3, or Ty-3a resistance genes from wild tomato species provide good to excellent resistance to most TYLCV strains. Resistant varieties remain the cornerstone of management in high-pressure regions and have allowed tomato production to continue in areas where TYLCV would otherwise make it impossible.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Integrated management combines resistant varieties, whitefly control, and sanitation. Whitefly control relies on systemic insecticides applied to transplants before field establishment using soil drench applications of neonicotinoids such as imidacloprid or thiamethoxam. This provides early-season protection during the most critical period. Foliar insecticide applications targeting whitefly nymphs on the underside of leaves are continued throughout the season. Reflective silver mulches repel whiteflies and reduce early infection rates. Yellow sticky traps monitor whitefly populations and trigger treatment thresholds. Insect-proof screen houses for transplant production prevent infection before field planting. Weed management around field perimeters reduces reservoir host populations.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
At transplanting, apply a systemic insecticide drench to all transplants. Place reflective mulch before transplanting. Install yellow sticky traps at the field perimeter immediately. When first curling and yellowing symptoms appear on plants, collect and destroy infected plants before additional whiteflies feed on them. Increase foliar insecticide applications targeting whitefly adults and nymphs. Assess the percentage of infected plants weekly to track epidemic progress.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Scout fields twice weekly from transplanting through harvest. Monitor whitefly populations using yellow sticky traps and direct leaf inspection on the underside of leaves. Assess plant symptoms beginning at the growing point and upper leaves where curling and yellowing first appear. Track the percentage of symptomatic plants in the field over time to assess epidemic rate. Inspect transplants before field planting for early symptoms that may indicate infection during greenhouse production.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus is most severe in tropical and subtropical tomato-producing regions including the Mediterranean basin, the Middle East, parts of Africa, South and Southeast Asia, and parts of the Americas including Florida and California. It is present wherever the silverleaf whitefly vector is established and where temperatures remain warm enough year-round to maintain whitefly populations. It is a primary constraint to tomato production in many warm-climate regions worldwide.
