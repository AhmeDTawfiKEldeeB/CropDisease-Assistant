# Powdery Mildew - Cherry

**Pathogen:** Podosphaera clandestina  
**Type:** Fungal  
**Host:** Cherry (Prunus avium, Prunus cerasus)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Powdery Mildew of cherry is a fungal disease caused by Podosphaera clandestina that attacks cherry trees in warm, dry climates with moderate humidity. Unlike most fungal diseases, it does not require free water for infection and is therefore most problematic in regions with warm days, cool nights, and moderate humidity rather than rainy conditions. The disease affects leaves, shoots, and fruit, producing a characteristic white powdery coating of fungal mycelium and spores on affected tissue. It is one of the most common diseases of sweet cherry worldwide and is a significant concern in commercial cherry production.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Powdery Mildew is rated as a moderate to high danger disease on cherry. It can cause significant economic losses through direct fruit infection, which results in premature cracking, surface russet, and poor appearance that reduces marketability. Heavy infection of young leaves and shoot tips can distort growth and reduce overall tree vigor. In seasons with favorable conditions, rapid spread through the canopy can occur within a few weeks. The disease is rarely fatal to established trees but has a substantial economic impact on fruit quality.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
The incubation period is five to seven days under optimal conditions. Unlike most fungal diseases, Powdery Mildew can spread rapidly during warm, dry weather because its spores do not require water for germination. The most critical infection window for fruit is from petal fall through pit hardening. The disease can spread continuously throughout the growing season under favorable conditions, with multiple overlapping generations of spore production occurring without interruption.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
Powdery Mildew overwinters as chasmothecia in bark crevices and as dormant mycelium in infected buds. In spring, ascospores released from chasmothecia provide primary inoculum. Once established, the fungus produces abundant conidia from white powdery colonies on leaf surfaces. These dry conidia are windborne and spread rapidly throughout the canopy and to neighboring trees. Unlike most fungal pathogens, Powdery Mildew spores are actually inhibited by free water on leaf surfaces, making it uniquely adapted to spread during dry weather. A single infected shoot tip can produce millions of conidia within days.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Podosphaera clandestina is an obligate biotrophic ascomycete that can only grow on living host tissue. It produces both a sexual stage with chasmothecia and an asexual stage with chains of conidia. The fungus grows on the surface of leaf tissue, penetrating only through specialized structures called haustoria that extract nutrients from epidermal cells. It does not penetrate deeply into plant tissue. The white powdery appearance results from the dense network of surface mycelium and conidial chains visible to the naked eye.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On leaves, Powdery Mildew produces white to grayish-white powdery patches on both the upper and lower surfaces. Young leaves may become cupped, twisted, or stunted when heavily infected. Severely infected leaves may yellow and drop prematurely. On shoot tips, white powdery growth covers expanding tissue and causes stunting and distortion of new growth. Infected shoot tips may fail to harden properly before winter, increasing cold damage risk. On fruit, white powdery patches develop on the skin surface that cause russet discoloration after the mycelium is rubbed away. Infected fruit may crack prematurely and are unmarketable. Calyx-end infections are common.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Powdery Mildew thrives in temperatures between ten and thirty degrees Celsius, with the optimum around twenty to twenty-five degrees. It does not require free water for spore germination and is uniquely adapted to infect during dry weather. High relative humidity of sixty-five to eighty percent promotes sporulation without rain. Conditions of warm days and cool nights create ideal humidity conditions. Dense, shaded canopies with poor airflow favor disease development by maintaining the humidity levels needed for sporulation.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
The cycle begins with overwintering inoculum in bark and infected buds. Primary spores released in spring infect young emerging tissue. Infected tissue rapidly produces conidia that spread through the canopy. Multiple generations of conidia are produced throughout the growing season without interruption under favorable conditions. In late summer and autumn, sexual chasmothecia form on infected tissue and overwinter in bark crevices to begin the cycle again the following spring.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
Cultural management focuses on improving canopy airflow through pruning to reduce the humidity conditions that favor sporulation. Removing infected shoot tips during summer pruning reduces inoculum within the orchard. Avoiding excessive nitrogen fertilization reduces the flush of highly susceptible succulent growth. Resistant varieties should be selected where possible. Overhead irrigation should be avoided in favor of drip irrigation systems. Variety selection plays a significant role. Some sweet cherry varieties such as Bing show moderate susceptibility while others show higher resistance. Sour cherry varieties are generally less susceptible than sweet cherry varieties.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Chemical control should begin at bud swell or at the first sign of disease and continue throughout the growing season during favorable conditions. Fungicide classes used include triazoles such as myclobutanil and tebuconazole, strobilurins such as azoxystrobin, SDHI products such as boscalid, and sulfur-based compounds in organic programs. Applications should be made on seven to fourteen day intervals during periods of active growth and favorable weather. Sulfur should not be applied when temperatures exceed thirty degrees Celsius to avoid phytotoxicity. Resistance management through mode of action rotation is essential given the continuous reproduction of the pathogen.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
At bud swell, begin monitoring for first infections on emerging tissue. When first white colonies appear on shoot tips or leaves, apply a systemic fungicide immediately and increase monitoring frequency. During rapid shoot growth periods with warm days and cool nights, do not allow spray intervals to exceed seven days. When fruit infections are detected, prioritize fungicide coverage and remove heavily infected fruit clusters. After harvest, conduct summer pruning to remove infected shoot tips and improve canopy airflow.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Scout weekly from bud swell through harvest, with particular attention to shoot tips and young leaves in the interior of the canopy. Assess the density of infected shoot tips after bloom as an indicator of disease pressure. Monitor weather conditions for warm days, cool nights, and moderate humidity that favor rapid spread. Interior canopy scouting is essential as these areas typically show disease first due to reduced airflow.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Powdery Mildew of cherry is most severe in warm, semi-arid cherry-growing regions including the Pacific Northwest of the United States, southern Europe, and parts of Australia. It is present in all cherry-producing regions worldwide but is less problematic in rainy climates where free water on leaf surfaces inhibits spore germination.
