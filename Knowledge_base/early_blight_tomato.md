# Early Blight - Tomato

**Pathogen:** Alternaria solani  
**Type:** Fungal  
**Host:** Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Early Blight is a fungal disease caused by Alternaria solani that attacks tomato plants throughout the growing season, particularly in warm, wet conditions. Despite its name, it can occur at any stage of plant development and is one of the most common and widespread foliar diseases of tomato worldwide. The disease causes distinctive target-like lesions on leaves, stems, and fruit that lead to progressive defoliation from the bottom of the plant upward. It is present in all tomato-producing regions and is favored by the warm temperatures and alternating wet and dry periods common in many tomato-growing climates.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Early Blight is rated as a moderate to high danger disease. While it rarely causes total crop failure in a single season, it can cause severe defoliation that reduces fruit quality through sunscald, decreases yield through reduced photosynthesis, and weakens plants making them more susceptible to other stresses. The progressive nature of the disease, beginning at the base of the plant and moving upward, means that if not managed it will eventually reach the upper canopy and affect fruit-bearing tissue. Economic losses of twenty to thirty percent are common in unmanaged susceptible varieties during wet seasons.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
The incubation period is three to five days under warm, humid conditions, and spore production begins rapidly after lesion formation. The disease is capable of multiple infection cycles per week under ideal conditions. Early in the season, spread is primarily from soil-splashed conidia infecting lower leaves. As the season progresses and the canopy fills, upper leaf spread via windborne conidia becomes more important. The disease progresses most rapidly during periods of warm temperatures combined with alternating wet and dry weather.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
Alternaria solani overwinters in infected plant debris and in the soil, where it can persist for extended periods. Conidia produced from infected debris are rain-splashed onto lower leaves, initiating primary infections early in the season. From these initial lesions, new conidia are produced and spread by wind and rain splash throughout the canopy. The pathogen can also be seedborne, allowing introduction via infected transplants or direct-seeded crops. Stressed plants, particularly those experiencing drought stress, nutrient deficiency, or heavy fruit loads, are significantly more susceptible than well-managed plants.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Alternaria solani is a deuteromycete fungus that produces large, distinctive club-shaped conidia with a long beak. It is a necrotrophic pathogen that kills host tissue in advance of colonization using cell wall-degrading enzymes and toxins. The fungus can survive for extended periods in soil and plant debris as dormant mycelium and spores. It has a wide host range within the Solanaceae family, also infecting potato, eggplant, and pepper. Multiple pathogen strains with varying levels of fungicide resistance exist in commercial production areas.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On leaves, Early Blight causes the disease's characteristic target-like lesions: dark brown, irregularly circular spots two to four centimeters in diameter with distinct concentric rings forming a bull's-eye pattern. A yellow halo surrounds mature lesions. Lesions first appear on older, lower leaves and progress upward through the canopy. Severely infected leaves turn yellow and drop, exposing fruit. On stems, the fungus causes brown, slightly sunken lesions at the soil line or on stem tissue, sometimes called collar rot when it infects the stem base of young transplants. On fruit, dark, sunken lesions develop near the stem end that have the same target-like concentric ring pattern as leaf lesions. Fruit lesions may become large and are often colonized by secondary pathogens.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Early Blight thrives in temperatures between twenty-four and thirty degrees Celsius but can cause infections across a wide range from fifteen to thirty-five degrees. The fungus requires free water on leaf surfaces for spore germination and infection. Alternating wet and dry periods are particularly favorable, as spores are produced during dry periods and dispersed effectively, then germinate when wet conditions return. Plant stress from drought, heavy fruit loads, or nutrient deficiencies significantly increases susceptibility.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
The cycle begins with the fungus overwintering in infected plant debris or soil. Conidia from debris infect lower leaves in early season via rain splash. Infected leaves produce abundant conidia that spread upward through the canopy during warm weather. The disease progresses from the bottom of the plant upward throughout the growing season. At the end of the season, infected tissue returns to the soil surface and serves as inoculum for the following season.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
Cultural management focuses on crop rotation with non-solanaceous crops for at least two years to reduce soilborne inoculum, removing and destroying infected plant debris after harvest, and using certified pathogen-free transplants. Mulching the soil surface reduces rain splash of conidia from soil and debris onto lower leaves. Stake and cage systems that keep foliage off the soil also reduce splash infection of lower leaves. Maintaining plant health through adequate irrigation and balanced nutrition reduces stress susceptibility. Variety selection offers some assistance. While no commercial variety is fully resistant, some varieties show moderate resistance that reduces disease severity compared to highly susceptible varieties.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Chemical control should begin when the first lesions appear or when weather conditions become favorable, whichever comes first. Fungicide classes used include multi-site protectants such as chlorothalonil, mancozeb, and copper; and single-site fungicides including strobilurins such as azoxystrobin and pyraclostrobin, and SDHI products. Applications should be made on seven to ten day intervals during favorable weather. Strobilurins should be rotated with other modes of action to manage resistance, as strobilurin-resistant Alternaria solani populations are documented in some production areas. Systemic fungicides provide both protective and limited curative activity within one to two days of infection.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
At transplanting, apply a protective fungicide to lower leaves and mulch the soil surface. When first lesions appear on lower leaves, immediately remove and destroy heavily infected lower leaves and apply a systemic fungicide. During warm, wet periods, maintain seven-day spray intervals without gaps. When defoliation reaches the middle canopy, assess the economic justification for continued management. At the end of the season, remove and destroy all infected plant tissue to reduce inoculum for the following season.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Scout fields weekly from transplanting through harvest. Begin inspection of the lowest leaves on the oldest plants in the field, as this is where the disease consistently appears first. Assess the percentage of leaves with lesions and the height to which the disease has progressed in the canopy relative to fruit-bearing tissue. Monitor plant stress indicators such as wilting, yellowing, or heavy fruit loads that increase susceptibility.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Early Blight of tomato is present in all major tomato-producing regions worldwide. It is most severe in regions with warm temperatures and alternating wet and dry weather, including the eastern United States, parts of Europe, and many tropical and subtropical tomato-growing regions. It is a year-round concern in tropical climates where tomatoes are grown continuously.
