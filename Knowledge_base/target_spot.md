# Target Spot - Tomato
Pathogen: Corynespora cassiicola | Type: Fungal | Host: Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum) and hundreds of other species


## Definition

Target Spot is a fungal disease of tomato caused by Corynespora cassiicola, a pathogen with one of the broadest host ranges of any plant pathogen, infecting over four hundred plant species across more than one hundred families. On tomato it produces circular to irregular brown lesions with concentric rings resembling a target or bullseye on leaves, stems, and fruit. Unlike Early Blight, which almost exclusively attacks older lower leaves, Target Spot infects leaves of all ages simultaneously, which is one of the key features distinguishing it in the field. It also infects fruit directly, a characteristic Early Blight lacks on tomato, making Target Spot capable of more direct economic damage to the marketable product. The disease has been increasing in importance globally over the past two decades, particularly in greenhouse production and tropical regions, partly due to the wide host range that provides persistent inoculum from many crop and non-crop sources in the environment.


## Severity and Danger Level

Target Spot is rated as moderate to high danger. It does not kill plants but severe defoliation combined with direct fruit infection can cause yield losses of twenty to fifty percent in severe cases. The damage from fruit lesions is particularly significant economically because it directly affects the marketable product. The disease is increasing in importance and was previously considered minor but is now recognized as a serious threat in many production systems worldwide.


## Time to Spread

The incubation period from infection to visible symptoms is five to seven days. The disease can affect plants at any growth stage and accumulates progressively over weeks. Fruit infection is most damaging when it occurs on green, expanding berries. The speed of spread is moderate but broader than many tomato diseases because the wide host range means inoculum is consistently available from multiple sources.


## How It Spreads

Target Spot overwinters in infected plant debris in the soil as conidia and mycelium. Conidia are released during wet weather and dispersed by wind and rain splash to leaves, stems, and fruit. The fungus penetrates through stomata or directly through the cuticle and can infect tissue of any age, unlike Septoria and Early Blight which preferentially infect older tissue. Between plants, wind-dispersed conidia carry the disease throughout the crop. The extremely wide host range of Corynespora cassiicola means that inoculum can originate from neighboring crops, ornamental plants, and weeds in addition to infected tomato debris. This makes inoculum management more complex than for tomato-specific pathogens because eliminating the disease from tomato debris does not eliminate the environmental inoculum pool. In greenhouses, workers and tools spread conidia and high humidity enables rapid spread between plants.


## Pathogen Biology

Corynespora cassiicola is an ascomycete fungus producing very distinctive conidia that are extremely large, up to five hundred micrometers long, multicellular, and cylindrical with a long tapering beak. Under a microscope the conidia are unmistakable and confirm identification definitively. The sexual stage is rarely observed in practice and the disease propagates almost entirely through asexual conidia. The pathogen survives in infected plant debris in soil, though its persistence is shorter than some other debris-inhabiting pathogens. Pathogenic races have been described based on virulence differences, though the race structure is less well characterized than in many other pathogens. Resistance to benzimidazole fungicides such as carbendazim has been documented in populations worldwide, and strobilurin resistance has also been reported, making fungicide rotation critical.


## Symptoms

On leaves, Target Spot produces circular to slightly irregular lesions with distinct concentric rings giving the target or bullseye appearance. Lesion size typically ranges from one to two centimeters and can be larger, which is substantially bigger than Septoria lesions. The color is brown to dark brown with a yellow halo. Unlike Early Blight which mainly affects older lower leaves, Target Spot infects leaves at all ages and positions in the canopy simultaneously. In advanced cases a gray center develops in lesions as the fungus sporulates on the dead tissue. Severe infection causes complete defoliation beginning in the lower canopy and progressing upward.

On fruit, Target Spot produces dark, sunken, circular lesions with the same concentric ring pattern seen on leaves. These lesions can penetrate into the flesh and create entry points for secondary bacterial and fungal rots. Significant direct fruit loss is possible, which distinguishes Target Spot from Early Blight and Septoria that rarely infect tomato fruit.

On stems and petioles, dark elongated lesions form and can girdle tissue causing dieback. Stem cankers are more commonly observed in greenhouse production where plants are larger and stem tissue is more exposed.


## Favorable Conditions

Target Spot thrives in temperatures between twenty-five and thirty-two degrees Celsius with an optimum around twenty-eight degrees. High humidity and extended leaf wetness periods are required for infection. The disease is most severe during warm, wet periods and in greenhouse and high tunnel production where high humidity is common. Dense canopies and overhead irrigation create ideal conditions for rapid spread.


## Disease Cycle

The cycle begins with conidia and mycelium surviving in infected plant debris in soil. Conidia are released during wet weather and dispersed by wind and rain splash. The fungus infects tissue of any age through stomata or direct cuticle penetration during humid periods. After five to seven days, new conidia are produced on lesion surfaces and wind and rain carry them throughout the crop. Fruit infection occurs throughout the season when conditions are favorable. At season end, infected debris returns to the soil to initiate the next season's epidemic. Because of the wide host range, conidia from non-tomato hosts in the environment contribute continuously to the inoculum pool.


## Treatment and Management

Crop rotation reduces debris-based inoculum but is less effective than for tomato-specific pathogens because the broad host range means inoculum can originate from many non-tomato sources. Rotating away from cucumbers, soybeans, and other highly susceptible crops is also worthwhile. Removing and destroying infected plant debris after harvest breaks the debris-based inoculum cycle. Improving air circulation through canopy management, pruning, and adequate plant spacing reduces the humidity microclimate favorable to infection. Drip irrigation should replace overhead systems to keep foliage dry. Infected lower leaves should be removed as disease progresses to reduce canopy inoculum. Surrounding crops and weeds should be scouted and managed to reduce alternate host sources of inoculum near the field. Greenhouse structures should be sanitized thoroughly between crops.

Truly resistant commercial tomato varieties are currently limited and active research is ongoing. Check regional variety trial data for tolerance ratings as new varieties become available.

Fungicide applications should be applied preventively when conditions favor infection or at the appearance of first symptoms. SDHI products including boscalid, fluopyram, and penthiopyrad provide excellent activity and are among the most effective options available. Strobilurins such as azoxystrobin and pyraclostrobin provide good protectant activity but resistance has been reported. Triazoles such as difenoconazole and tebuconazole are systemic with curative activity. Multi-site protectants including chlorothalonil and mancozeb are important components of any program for resistance management. Benzimidazoles such as carbendazim should be avoided or minimized because resistance is documented globally. Application intervals of seven to ten days are typical, shortening to five to seven days during wet weather. Good coverage of the entire plant including fruit clusters and stem tissue is important. Modes of action must be rotated with every application to prevent resistance. A practical rotation would cycle through chlorothalonil, then SDHI plus triazole premix, then strobilurin plus mancozeb, then back to chlorothalonil.

In organic programs, copper-based fungicides provide moderate protectant activity. Bacillus subtilis products have some suppressive activity. Cultural controls including rotation, canopy management, drip irrigation, and debris removal are the foundation of organic Target Spot management.


## Quick Actions

When bullseye spots appear on leaves of all ages rather than only old lower leaves, apply a systemic fungicide immediately and remove severely infected leaves to confirm Target Spot rather than Early Blight. When fruit shows circular sunken lesions, spray immediately with an SDHI or strobilurin product and remove infected fruit. When disease appears in a greenhouse with high humidity, improve ventilation urgently, apply fungicide, and remove infected tissue. When disease is appearing simultaneously in multiple crop types nearby, scout for alternate host sources and consider expanding the spray program coverage.


## Scouting and Monitoring

Begin scouting soon after transplanting because Target Spot can appear at any plant stage. Check leaves of all ages at every scouting visit, because unlike Early Blight or Septoria, young leaves are also susceptible and this is the key field diagnostic feature. Inspect fruit clusters carefully because fruit lesions may appear before canopy defoliation becomes severe. Record the percentage of plants affected, which leaf ages are affected, and the percentage of fruit showing lesions. In greenhouses, pay attention to stem lesions at plant base and mid-stem. High-risk weather periods are temperatures between twenty-five and thirty-two degrees Celsius combined with overhead irrigation, rain, or dense fog.


## Geographic Distribution

Target Spot was originally considered a tropical and subtropical disease but has expanded in importance globally, particularly in protected production environments. It is now significant in Southeast Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, the Caribbean and Latin America, the southern United States including Florida and Gulf Coast states, Mediterranean greenhouse production, and northern European greenhouse cucumber and tomato production.


## Frequently Asked Questions

How do I tell Target Spot from Early Blight? Target Spot affects leaves of all ages while Early Blight mainly affects old lower leaves. Target Spot also frequently infects fruit with sunken circular lesions while Early Blight rarely infects tomato fruit directly. Both have bullseye ring patterns on leaves, making the leaf age distribution the key practical distinction. Laboratory confirmation may be needed for certainty.

Does it infect other crops? Yes. Corynespora cassiicola has an extremely wide host range including cucumbers, papaya, beans, soybeans, and many ornamentals. Practice good field hygiene between crops and scout for alternate inoculum sources nearby.

Is this disease getting worse over time? Yes. Target Spot has gained importance globally over the past two decades, likely due to a combination of warmer temperatures, expansion of protected production, and possibly increased fungicide resistance in pathogen populations reducing the effectiveness of chemical programs that previously kept it in check.

My fungicide does not seem to be working against Target Spot. What should I do? Resistance to carbendazim and strobilurins is documented in Corynespora cassiicola. Switch to an SDHI-containing product such as boscalid or fluopyram combined with a multi-site protectant such as chlorothalonil or mancozeb. Consult a local plant pathologist if control failures continue.

Does it spread in dry conditions? Spread is much slower in dry conditions because the fungus requires high humidity and leaf wetness for infection. Hot, dry wind can carry conidia over long distances but infection only occurs when humidity rises and leaf surfaces become wet.
