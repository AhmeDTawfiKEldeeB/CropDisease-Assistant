# Late Blight - Potato
Pathogen: Phytophthora infestans | Type: Oomycete (Water Mold) | Host: Potato (Solanum tuberosum), also Tomato


## Definition

Potato Late Blight is the most dangerous potato disease in the world, caused by Phytophthora infestans, an oomycete or water mold rather than a true fungus. It is historically responsible for the Irish Famine of 1845 to 1852, during which it destroyed the potato crop across Ireland causing over one million deaths and the emigration of two million more. Today it remains an active and serious threat, costing global agriculture more than six billion dollars annually. The disease creates large, dark, water-soaked spots on leaves with white cottony mold underneath, and causes reddish-brown rot inside tubers that can spread through an entire storage facility. It spreads with extreme speed, capable of destroying an entire field in one to two weeks and traveling by wind for kilometers to infect neighboring farms. Because it is an oomycete and not a true fungus, most standard fungicides have no effect on it, and only oomycete-active products can provide control.


## Severity and Danger Level

Late Blight is rated as critical, the highest possible danger level for any plant disease. It can kill entire plants and entire fields within seven to fourteen days under favorable conditions. Yield losses can reach one hundred percent in the field, with additional storage losses when infected tubers are mixed with healthy ones. The speed of destruction is extreme, making it unlike any other common crop disease. Any confirmed sighting in the field should be treated as an immediate emergency.


## Time to Spread

The incubation period is just two to four days under cool, wet conditions, one of the shortest of any major crop disease. An entire field can collapse within seven to fourteen days of first infection under favorable weather. Spores travel by wind for kilometers, meaning a single infected field can endanger every potato farm in the surrounding region. Infected tubers spread disease to healthy tubers in storage, and an entire storage bin can be lost in weeks from a single infected lot.


## How It Spreads

The primary overwintering source is infected potato tubers, whether left in the field as volunteers, stored in facilities, or discarded in cull piles near the field. These infected tubers begin sporulating under cool, wet spring conditions and release spores that are carried by wind. Rain releases and disperses sporangia from infected plant material. Zoospores released from sporangia in the presence of water infect tubers through lenticels and wounds when sporangia are washed into the soil by rain. In storage, infected tubers produce mycelium that spreads to adjacent healthy tubers, and secondary bacterial soft rots rapidly accelerate the destruction. Cull piles of discarded infected potatoes left near fields are a major and often overlooked inoculum source that should always be destroyed immediately.


## Pathogen Biology

Phytophthora infestans is an oomycete, biologically more similar to algae and diatoms than to true fungi. This evolutionary distance is why fungicides targeting fungal-specific processes such as ergosterol biosynthesis have no activity against it. Only oomycete-specific products work. The pathogen produces sporangia that can either germinate directly at cool temperatures or release biflagellate zoospores at warmer temperatures, giving it flexibility across a range of conditions. Where both A1 and A2 mating types are present, sexual reproduction produces oospores that survive in soil for multiple years, complicating long-term management. Metalaxyl and mefenoxam resistance is extremely common worldwide and must be assumed unless specifically proven otherwise in a given region. The pathogen continues to evolve, and new genetic lineages emerge regularly that can overcome host resistance genes.


## Symptoms

On leaves, Late Blight produces large, irregular, water-soaked lesions that begin pale green and rapidly transition to dark brown or black necrosis with an irregular pale yellow border. In humid conditions, white cottony sporulation appears on the underside of infected leaves, and this is the key diagnostic sign requiring immediate action. Lesions expand visibly day by day and the entire plant can collapse within three to five days. A distinct musty or earthy odor is often noticed in heavily infected fields.

On stems, dark brown to black lesions progress rapidly and cause complete stem death, collapsing the entire shoot above the infection point.

On tubers, the disease produces reddish-brown discoloration beginning just beneath the skin, extending inward as a firm, dry rot with an irregular pattern. This is distinct from bacterial soft rots, which are watery and strongly odorous. Infected tubers in storage attract secondary bacterial invasion and can liquefy entirely. One infected tuber in a storage bin can spread disease to the entire lot.


## Favorable Conditions

Late Blight thrives in temperatures between ten and twenty-five degrees Celsius with an optimum around eighteen degrees. It requires greater than ninety percent relative humidity for at least twelve hours to produce spores effectively. Cool nights with dew and frequent rainfall are the classic warning conditions. Forecasting systems including the Hutton Criteria, which define infection risk as two consecutive days with minimum temperatures above ten degrees and relative humidity above ninety percent for at least six hours, are used by growers to anticipate outbreaks before symptoms appear.


## Disease Cycle

Primary inoculum comes from infected tubers overwintering in soil as volunteers, from unharvested cull piles, and from storage facilities. These sources begin sporulating under cool, wet spring conditions. Sporangia are dispersed by wind and rain to potato foliage. Zoospores or directly germinating sporangia infect through stomata and wounds. After an incubation of just two to four days, the plant begins sporulating itself, amplifying the inoculum exponentially. Rain washes sporangia into soil where zoospores infect tubers through lenticels. Infected tubers at harvest carry disease into storage, where it spreads to healthy lots. Where oospores form from sexual reproduction, they survive in soil for years and complicate rotation-based management.


## Treatment

Planting only certified disease-free seed potatoes is the most fundamental preventive measure and is not optional. Infected seed is the most direct way to introduce the disease into a clean field. Resistant varieties with multiple resistance genes, particularly those with quantitative multigenic resistance, significantly reduce epidemic severity and are available through regional variety programs. Even resistant varieties require some fungicide protection in high-pressure seasons. Volunteer potato plants in and around fields serve as green bridges for the pathogen and must be destroyed. Cull piles must be buried deeply or composted far from fields immediately after sorting. Harvest should occur only when haulm is fully dead and tuber skins are fully set to minimize tuber infection during harvest operations.

## Management

Chemical control must use only oomycete-active fungicides. Protectant fungicides including chlorothalonil, mancozeb, and copper coat the tissue surface and prevent infection but have no curative activity. They must be reapplied frequently, especially after rain. Systemic fungicides with curative activity include dimethomorph, fluopicolide, mandipropamid, cymoxanil, and propamocarb. These penetrate tissue and can stop infections in progress for a limited window of twenty-four to forty-eight hours. Metalaxyl and mefenoxam are systemic but resistance is extremely common worldwide and they should never be used as solo products, only in premix with a contact fungicide. The spray program should begin preventively using forecasting criteria, not after symptoms appear. During high-risk weather, applications every five to seven days are required. An example rotation would cycle through mancozeb alone, then mandipropamid plus mancozeb, then chlorothalonil alone, then cymoxanil plus mancozeb, then fluopicolide plus propamocarb, repeating without ever using the same mode of action in consecutive applications.

Storage management is as important as field management. All tubers should be carefully inspected before storage. Only healthy, dry, properly cured tubers should enter storage. Curing at twelve to fifteen degrees Celsius for ten to fourteen days allows wound healing before final storage temperature. Storage should be maintained at four to seven degrees Celsius with good ventilation and humidity around ninety percent. Regular inspection of stored lots is essential and any showing rot must be removed immediately before it spreads.

In organic programs, copper-based fungicides are the primary chemical tool. Potassium phosphite provides systemic activity and some curative effect and is approved in some organic programs. Resistant varieties and extremely tight copper spray schedules of five to seven days during wet weather are the foundation of organic Late Blight management.


## Quick Actions

When disease is reported in the surrounding region, spray immediately without waiting for symptoms in your own field. When cool, foggy, or rainy weather is forecast, apply a systemic fungicide twenty-four hours before the weather event. When white mold appears on leaf undersides, this is an emergency requiring action within hours, not days. Remove all visibly infected plants, spray the entire field with a systemic plus protectant combination, and stop all overhead irrigation immediately. Alert neighboring farms because spores travel kilometers by wind. At harvest, inspect all tubers carefully and do not store any showing infection.


## Scouting and Monitoring

Scout fields twice weekly from canopy closure through harvest during high-risk seasons. Focus first on field edges, low-lying areas, and areas near irrigation sources or volunteer plants. Check leaf undersides carefully because white sporulation appears there before upper surface symptoms are obvious. Use weather monitoring and disease forecasting tools to predict risk periods. Register for regional Late Blight alerts through local agricultural extension or plant health services. After any confirmed case is reported in the region, increase scouting to daily inspections.


## Geographic Distribution

Late Blight is present on every continent where potatoes are grown. It is most severe in cool, wet climates including Ireland, the United Kingdom, northern Europe, the Pacific Northwest of the United States, the Andes region where the pathogen originated, and highland Africa. New, more aggressive genetic lineages including US-8 and several EU lineages have spread globally since the 1990s and have complicated resistance management in many regions.
