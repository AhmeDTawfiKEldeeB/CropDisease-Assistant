# Tomato Mosaic Virus - Tomato

**Pathogen:** Tomato mosaic virus (ToMV)  
**Type:** Viral  
**Host:** Tomato (Solanum lycopersicum)

## definition
This section is about the definition of the disease.
Tomato Mosaic Virus is a viral disease caused by Tomato mosaic virus, a member of the Tobamovirus genus, that infects tomato plants worldwide. It is one of the most stable and persistent plant viruses known, capable of surviving for years in dried plant debris, on contaminated tools, and on worker hands and clothing. The disease causes mottling, mosaic patterning, and distortion of leaves, reduces plant vigor, and can cause fruit deformation and color abnormalities that reduce marketability. It spreads primarily through mechanical contact rather than insect vectors, making field hygiene and contamination prevention the central elements of management.

## danger
This section is about the severity of the disease.
Tomato Mosaic Virus is rated as a moderate to high danger disease. Yield losses of ten to twenty-five percent are common in infected fields, and losses can exceed fifty percent when infection occurs early in the growing season and affects a high proportion of plants. The virus reduces photosynthetic efficiency, distorts fruit development, and can cause internal browning of fruit tissue that renders it unmarketable even when external appearance seems acceptable. The extraordinary persistence of the virus in the environment makes it particularly challenging to eradicate once a production facility is contaminated.

## timeline
This section is about the timeline and spread speed of the disease.
Tomato Mosaic Virus spreads extremely rapidly through mechanical contact, potentially reaching every plant in a greenhouse within hours if proper precautions are not taken. In the field, spread occurs more slowly through worker movement and equipment but can still progress through a planting within days during transplanting or pruning operations. The speed of spread is directly correlated with the number of human contacts made with infected and then healthy plants.

## spread
This section is about how the disease spreads.
Tomato Mosaic Virus spreads primarily through mechanical contact, which distinguishes it from most other plant viruses that require insect vectors. The virus is present in infected plant sap and is transmitted when contaminated hands, tools, clothing, or equipment contact healthy plant tissue through wounds, stomata, or trichome damage during normal handling. It can survive in dried plant tissue for years, in tobacco products for extended periods, and on contaminated surfaces in greenhouses for weeks to months. Seed transmission occurs at low rates from infected plants. The virus does not spread through soil or water and does not require an insect vector for spread.

## pathogen
This section is about the biology of the pathogen.
Tomato mosaic virus is a positive-sense single-stranded RNA virus with a rod-shaped virion structure characteristic of the Tobamovirus genus. It is closely related to Tobacco mosaic virus and shares many biological characteristics including extraordinary environmental stability. The virus replicates in the cytoplasm of infected cells and moves systemically through the phloem to infect all parts of the plant. It produces very high concentrations of virus particles in infected tissue, making even small amounts of infected sap highly infectious. The virus does not require a biological vector for transmission.

## symptoms
This section is about the symptoms of the disease.
On leaves, Tomato Mosaic Virus causes a distinctive mosaic or mottling pattern of light and dark green areas that follow a non-uniform distribution across the leaf blade. Leaves may show various degrees of distortion, including curling, bubbling, or narrowing depending on strain and plant age at infection. On young plants infected early, severe stunting and distortion of the entire plant may occur. Growing points may show light green to yellow coloration and distorted leaf development. On fruit, external symptoms include uneven ripening with green or yellow patches on otherwise ripe fruit. Internal browning of fruit wall tissue, visible when fruit is cut, can occur with some virus strains and renders fruit commercially worthless despite normal external appearance.

## conditions
This section is about the favorable conditions for the disease.
Tomato Mosaic Virus does not have specific weather-related favorable conditions for spread the way fungal and bacterial pathogens do, because it does not require moisture for transmission. Spread is driven by human activity: transplanting, pruning, trellising, harvesting, and other operations that involve repeated plant contact. Greenhouses and high-density production systems where workers contact many plants rapidly are particularly high-risk environments. Operations that employ workers who use tobacco products face additional risk due to virus survival in tobacco.

## cycle
This section is about the disease cycle.
Unlike fungal and bacterial diseases, Tomato Mosaic Virus does not have a seasonal cycle driven by weather. Once introduced into a planting, infected plants remain infected and serve as continuous inoculum sources throughout the season and potentially longer through contaminated debris. The virus persists in the environment between crops and can reinfect new plantings from contaminated surfaces, tools, and debris. Seed from infected plants can introduce the virus to new, otherwise clean production sites.

## treatment
This section is about the treatment of the disease.
There is no curative treatment for virus-infected plants. Management is entirely based on prevention and containment. Confirmed infected plants should be removed and destroyed immediately to reduce inoculum within the field or greenhouse. Roguing infected plants is most effective when done early, before secondary spread has occurred. Variety selection is the most effective long-term management strategy. Many commercial tomato varieties carry the Tm-2 or Tm-22 resistance gene that provides good to excellent resistance to most strains of Tomato Mosaic Virus. These resistance genes have been incorporated into most commercial tomato breeding programs and are widely available.

## management
This section is about the management of the disease.
Management of Tomato Mosaic Virus relies entirely on prevention and sanitation. Key practices include using certified virus-free seed or hot water-treated seed, selecting resistant varieties, disinfecting all tools and equipment between plants during pruning and harvesting operations using ten percent bleach solution or other registered disinfectants, requiring workers to wash hands with soap and water before entering the crop and between rows, prohibiting tobacco use in or near the greenhouse or field, removing and destroying infected plants immediately, and disinfecting greenhouses between crops. No chemical treatment has any activity against the virus in infected plants.

## actions
This section is about quick actions to take.
When mosaic symptoms are first detected, immediately isolate suspected infected plants and submit samples for laboratory confirmation. Upon confirmation, remove and bag infected plants without contacting other plants and destroy them outside the production area. Disinfect all tools, work surfaces, and worker hands and clothing. Identify the probable source of introduction and correct the practice or condition that allowed it. Increase roguing intensity to identify additional infected plants before further spread occurs.

## scouting
This section is about monitoring and scouting.
Scout plants weekly from transplanting through harvest, with particular attention at transplanting when mechanical spread risk is highest. Examine growing points and young leaves for mosaic patterns, distortion, and color abnormalities. In greenhouses, walk rows in a systematic pattern and mark any suspicious plants for follow-up observation or sampling. Maintain records of where infected plants are found to identify patterns that suggest the source or route of spread.

## geography
This section is about the geographic distribution of the disease.
Tomato Mosaic Virus is present in all tomato-producing regions worldwide. It is most problematic in greenhouse production systems where dense plant populations and frequent human contact create ideal conditions for mechanical spread. It is a universal threat wherever tomatoes are grown, with no geographic region being free from risk where contaminated seed, transplants, or production equipment are present.
