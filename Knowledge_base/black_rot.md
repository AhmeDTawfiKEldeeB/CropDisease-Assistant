# Black Rot - Grape
Pathogen: Guignardia bidwellii | Type: Fungal | Host: Grapevine (Vitis spp.)


## Definition

Black Rot is a fungal disease caused by Guignardia bidwellii that attacks grapevines in warm, humid climates. It is one of the most economically damaging diseases in viticulture, capable of destroying an entire harvest within a single season if left unmanaged. The disease affects leaves, shoots, and berries, but its most devastating and characteristic effect is on the fruit, which turns completely black and shrivels into hard, mummified balls that remain attached to the cluster. It is widespread in humid grape-growing regions worldwide and is considered a primary threat to European wine grape varieties.


## Severity and Danger Level

Black Rot is rated as a very high danger disease. It does not kill the grapevine itself, but it can destroy one hundred percent of the fruit crop in a single season under warm, wet conditions. Yield losses of this magnitude have been documented in unmanaged vineyards. The speed of damage is fast once infection begins, with berries moving from healthy to fully mummified within one to two weeks. The economic impact is among the highest of any grape disease worldwide, affecting both fresh market and wine grape production.


## Time to Spread

The incubation period ranges from eight to twenty-five days depending on temperature, with the fastest development at around twenty-six degrees Celsius. The most critical infection window is from bloom through four weeks after bloom, during which young berries are highly susceptible. Once a berry is infected, visible symptoms appear within one to three weeks, and mummification follows within another one to two weeks. The disease spreads fastest during warm, wet spring and early summer weather coinciding with the post-bloom period.


## How It Spreads

Black Rot overwinters in mummified berries and infected canes left from the previous season. In spring, rainfall triggers the release of ascospores from overwintering structures in the old mummies and infected wood. These spores are carried by wind and rain splash to young leaves, shoots, and developing berries. Young berries are the most vulnerable tissue and can be infected from bloom until about four weeks after bloom. After primary infections establish, the fungus produces secondary spores called conidia from new lesions, which continue spreading through the season via wind and rain. Mummified berries left on the vine or on the ground are the single most important source of inoculum for the following season.


## Pathogen Biology

Guignardia bidwellii is an ascomycete fungus with two reproductive stages. The sexual stage produces ascospores inside overwintering structures called pseudothecia, which form in mummified berries and infected canes. These ascospores are the primary inoculum released in spring during rainfall. The asexual stage produces conidia from pycnidia that form in leaf lesions during the growing season, providing secondary inoculum throughout the summer. The fungus penetrates host tissue through the cuticle and stomata and requires free water on the tissue surface for infection. It does not persist in soil.


## Symptoms

On leaves, Black Rot produces small yellowish spots that enlarge into reddish-brown circular lesions between two and ten millimeters in diameter. Each lesion has a dark brown border and contains small black pycnidia arranged in concentric rings inside the lesion. Severely infected leaves may drop prematurely.

On shoots and petioles, the fungus causes dark brown to black elongated lesions that become sunken and shriveled. These can girdle and kill young shoots when infection is severe.

On berries, small white spots appear first, then the berry turns entirely brown and then black. The infected berry shrivels into a hard, black mummified form that remains attached to the cluster. This mummification is the hallmark of Black Rot and is unmistakable once recognized.

On cluster stems and tendrils, dark shrunken lesions can cut off water supply to entire bunches, accelerating berry collapse.


## Favorable Conditions

Black Rot thrives in temperatures between ten and thirty-two degrees Celsius, with the optimum around twenty-six degrees. The fungus requires a minimum of four hours of free water on the tissue surface to complete infection. At twenty-six degrees this threshold is met after just six hours of wetness, while at ten degrees it requires twenty-four hours. The disease is most severe during warm, humid summers with frequent rainfall, especially when these conditions coincide with the bloom and post-bloom period.


## Disease Cycle

The cycle begins with the fungus overwintering inside mummified berries and infected canes. Spring rains trigger the release of ascospores, which infect young tissue during wet periods. After an incubation of eight to twenty-five days, lesions form on leaves and shoots. Pycnidia within these lesions release conidia that spread through wind and rain for secondary infections throughout the season. Berries become infected primarily during the critical post-bloom window. Infected berries mummify and either remain on the vine or fall to the ground, carrying the fungus into the next season.


## Treatment

Cultural management begins with the removal and destruction of all mummified berries during dormant pruning. This single action has the greatest impact on reducing next season's inoculum. Infected canes should also be removed. Canopy airflow should be improved through shoot positioning, leaf removal, and proper training systems to reduce humidity and accelerate drying after rain. Overhead irrigation should be avoided, and fallen leaf material should be cleared from the vineyard floor after harvest.

Variety selection plays a significant role. American grape varieties such as Concord and Niagara have moderate natural resistance, while most European Vitis vinifera varieties including Chardonnay, Merlot, and Cabernet Sauvignon are highly susceptible. Disease-resistant hybrid varieties such as Marquette and Frontenac offer good to excellent resistance and are valuable where variety flexibility exists.

## Management

Chemical control should begin at budbreak with a protectant fungicide and continue on a seven-day interval from bloom through four weeks post-bloom. This is the most critical spray window and should not be skipped. After the critical window, intervals can extend to ten to fourteen days in dry weather. Fungicide classes used include multi-site protectants such as mancozeb and captan, systemic triazoles such as myclobutanil and tebuconazole, strobilurins such as azoxystrobin, and SDHI products such as boscalid. Modes of action must be rotated every two to three sprays to prevent fungicide resistance. Strobilurins should never be used alone and should always be mixed with a protectant. In organic programs, copper-based fungicides and captan are the primary options and must be applied on tight schedules given their purely protective activity.


## Quick Actions

If no symptoms are present before bloom, apply a protective fungicide and remove any old mummies immediately. During the bloom period in wet weather, spray every seven days without exception. When first leaf spots appear, spray immediately and inspect all berries closely. When first infected berries are spotted, remove the infected clusters and spray remaining clusters with a systemic fungicide. After harvest, remove all mummies from vines and ground and destroy them.


## Scouting and Monitoring

Scout weekly from budbreak through six weeks post-bloom. Focus inspection on berries, leaf undersides, and shoot tips. Assess how thoroughly mummies were removed before the season, as incompletely cleaned vineyards carry dramatically higher risk. After rain events during the critical window, inspect within three to five days for the first leaf lesions. Use disease forecasting models to optimize spray timing where available.


## Geographic Distribution

Black Rot is most severe in humid continental climates, particularly in the eastern United States and central Europe. It is present wherever grapevines are grown commercially but causes less damage in dry Mediterranean and Pacific Coast climates where warm, wet spring conditions are less common.
